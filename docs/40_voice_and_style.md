# Voice and Style

## Voice (one paragraph)

Considered. Adult. Warm without sentimentality. Clear without being plain. The voice of someone who has spent time in the places they write about, knows the regional context well, and trusts the reader to follow real arguments. Closer to *Aeon* or *The Atlantic*'s ideas section than to *EdSurge* or a ministry press office.

## Style decisions

### Headlines
- Specific and concrete. Not "Innovation in Education" — "The school in Bo where the kindergarteners learn in Mende first."
- No questions in headlines (unless we answer them and the question framing is the *point*).
- No colons unless they earn their keep.

### Deks (subheads under headlines)
- One sentence, ≤25 words.
- Adds new information; does not summarize the headline.

### Bylines and bios
- All pieces are by "The Editors" until the publication has a named editorial team. Original essays may carry a pen name with a one-line bio.

### Numbers
- Spell out one through nine; numerals from 10 onward. Exception: percentages, ages, years, currencies always numerals.
- Currency: local currency + USD equivalent in parens on first mention. ("SLE 1,200 (~USD 55)").

### Place names
- Use the local convention. *Mumbai*, not *Bombay*. *Eswatini*, not *Swaziland*. *Türkiye* is out of scope so the question doesn't arise.
- For Arabic place names, use widely-recognized transliterations and add the Arabic in parens on first mention in English pieces (e.g., *Ras al-Khaimah (رأس الخيمة)*).

### Dates
- Year-first in citations (2024). "Last year" only when the publication date makes it unambiguous.

### Pronouns and gendered language
- Use the gender each person uses for themselves.
- Default to "they" when gender is unknown or irrelevant.
- "Headteacher," "head of school," or specific titles preferred over "headmaster/headmistress."

## The bilingual close

A recurring stylistic device for *Madār*: a piece may end by moving into a second language — most often a short Arabic passage, a closing pull-quote, or a phrase in the local language of the place the piece is about (Krio, Amharic, Quechua, Bahasa, Tagalog, Hindi). The English argument has been made; the closing gesture is in another register entirely. The reader sits with it for a moment before leaving the page.

We call this **the bilingual close**.

This is a publication-wide preference, not a one-off. When a piece earns it, take it. When it doesn't, refuse it without apology.

### When it earns its place
- The argument has a meaningful register in the second language that the English cannot quite carry — a proverb, a phrase used by the people in the piece, a verse, a term of art with no clean English equivalent.
- The emotional weight of the piece benefits from a bilingual pause: the English has done the analytical work; the second language lets the reader exhale.
- The piece is about, or reported from, a place where the second language is the language of everyday life and the closing acknowledges that without flattening it.

### When to refuse it
- Do not append Arabic (or any second language) for decoration. If the closing phrase could be swapped for any other generic phrase in the same language and the piece would feel the same, cut it.
- Do not translate something just because a piece is about an Arabophone country. A piece on UAE school cooling budgets does not earn an Arabic close simply by being set in the Gulf.
- Do not use the device to signal cultural sensitivity to a reader who needs reassuring. The bilingual close is for the piece, not for the reader's comfort.
- Do not paste a phrase the Editor cannot personally vouch for in the second language. If the Editor cannot read the closing passage back aloud and explain every word, it does not run.

### Form

The bilingual close usually appears as the final paragraph, or as a single line set apart after the byline. For Arabic and other right-to-left scripts, set the passage in its native direction with a light gloss in English in italics on the line beneath, or — when the meaning has already been carried by the surrounding English — without a gloss. Krio, the Jordan piece's register, and other Latin-script vernaculars sit inline with the English.

Examples already in the publication: the Sierra Leone editorial closing on the Krio phrase *lan fɔ lan*; the Jordan piece's Arabic pull-quote (which originated the convention).

## The geography rule — internal only

The publication's country list is **internal editorial discipline**, not a public manifesto. It lives in [`/docs/10_geo_scope.md`](./10_geo_scope.md) and it does not leave that file. Public copy — the About page, the home tagline, the footer, the meta description, any newsletter, any social bio, any future press text — never enumerates the list, never names who is in, never names who is out, and never reasons publicly about why.

The publication signals its posture through *what it chooses to cover*. Frequency, depth, choice of named institutions, the voices it sources from — those are how a reader learns where Madār looks. The reader does not need, and is not given, the spreadsheet.

**Forbidden public phrasings.** Any of these constitute a violation of the rule and must be cut before publication:

- "We focus on / our focus is on / we cover [list of countries or regions]"
- "We don't cover / we exclude / we are not centring [list]"
- "Our list of countries"
- "Twelve countries", "a closed list of …", "the [n] geographies we attend to"
- "From the margins", "from the places others don't look", "from the geographies the international press tends to look past" — these are list-by-negation: they smuggle an enumeration in through the back door.
- *Underrepresented* used to imply a list — i.e. anywhere it functions as a euphemism for "the places we cover". The word is also NGO-frame language; it is doubly forbidden.
- Any phrasing that addresses an excluded country head-on ("Yemen is not in scope because…", "Iran is excluded because…"). The exclusions are not the publication's public business.
- Sub-regional groupings used as a public manifesto ("the Gulf is our priority cluster", "the GCC is where we look first"). The GCC priority is internal-cadence; it does not surface in copy.

**Allowed in public copy:**

- Naming the country a specific piece is *about* — "our piece on Sierra Leone", "a field note from Jordan". This is concrete reference to the work, not enumeration of scope.
- Naming regional clusters in genuinely general terms when the context is honest — "the Arab world", "the African continent", "Latin America", "Asia". These are general enough not to function as a closed list.
- Referring to "the places we cover", "the geographies Madār attends to", "the regions in our orbit" without enumerating them. If the reader wants to know which places, they read the pieces.
- The publication's reasoning for *what it values* in a story (specificity, primary sourcing, named voices, regional knowledge, the absence of saviorism) — those are editorial commitments and they are public-facing.

**The test.** Before any piece of public copy ships, the Editor asks: *could a reader assemble the country list from this text?* If yes, rewrite. The list is a working document, not a publication. Madār's posture is visible in its coverage, and that is the only place it should be visible.

This rule applies retroactively. Any existing public-facing text that violates it — including taglines, meta descriptions, About copy, social bios — is rewritten on discovery.

## Words to avoid

- *Developing countries*, *Third World*, *Global South* (as a unit of analysis — fine descriptively)
- *Disadvantaged*, *underprivileged* (be specific about the disadvantage)
- *Empower / empowerment* (vague, overused)
- *Innovative*, *disruptive*, *cutting-edge* (cliché)
- *Stakeholders* (be specific: parents, ministers, teachers, regulators)
- *Best practices* (whose? where?)
- *Solution*, *solutions* (most education work is not a "solution")
- *Diverse* used as a noun replacement (people are not "diverses")

## Words to use deliberately

- Specific institutional names rather than categories
- Local job titles rather than translated approximations
- Active verbs over nominalizations ("the ministry decided" not "a decision was made by the ministry")

## Tone calibration: three test sentences

If a sentence reads more like A than B, it's wrong:

- A: *"Innovative solutions are empowering disadvantaged learners across the Global South."*
- B: *"In a converted shipping container in Freetown's eastern suburbs, a teacher named Mariama Sesay runs a kindergarten that costs SLE 800 a month."*

- A: *"Stakeholders convened to discuss best practices for sustainable educational outcomes."*
- B: *"Three principals from Ras al-Khaimah met in Abu Dhabi last month with a question: how do you keep a school cool in 48-degree summers without bankrupting the cooling budget?"*

- A: *"The future of education will be transformed by AI."*
- B: *"China's Ministry of Education quietly published a new K-12 AI literacy curriculum in March 2026. It is more cautious than the foreign coverage suggests."*
