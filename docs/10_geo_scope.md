# Geographic Scope — strict rules (internal)

This is the geographic spine of the publication. Treat the rules as binding. Any expansion or contraction is a founder decision (escalate to Manager → Vini), not an editorial judgment call.

## In scope — MENA (closed list)

Only these twelve Middle Eastern / North African countries are in scope:

- United Arab Emirates
- Saudi Arabia
- Qatar
- Bahrain
- Jordan
- Palestine
- Lebanon
- Syria
- Kuwait
- Oman
- Iraq *(added 2026-05-25 by Vini)*
- Egypt *(added 2026-05-25 by Vini)*

**Not in scope** in MENA: Iran, Israel, Morocco, Tunisia, Algeria, Libya, **Yemen**, Turkey. Do not publish on these countries even if a story is compelling. If a story about an in-scope country mentions one of these in passing, that is fine; the *subject* of the piece must remain an in-scope country.

> **Note on Yemen (2026-05-25):** When Iraq and Egypt were added, Yemen was explicitly considered and excluded by the founder. Editor should not propose Yemen-anchored pieces.

## GCC priority cluster (internal — editorial-calendar weight)

Within MENA, the six GCC countries — **United Arab Emirates, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman** — are the **priority cluster** for editorial-calendar purposes. They receive disproportionate weight when the Editor proposes pieces, sequences a month's calendar, or chooses between competing candidates of roughly equal editorial merit. GCC-anchored pieces should outnumber non-GCC MENA pieces over any given calendar quarter, and the Editor should actively cultivate sources, primary documents, and named regional voices in GCC ministries, schools, and research institutions.

This is an internal cadence rule, not a public framing. Madār never publishes that the GCC is its priority cluster, never publishes a "we focus on the Gulf" line, and never groups its coverage publicly by sub-region. The signal travels through frequency of coverage, choice of named institutions, and depth of sourcing — not through a manifesto statement.

The non-GCC in-scope MENA countries — Jordan, Palestine, Lebanon, Syria, Iraq, Egypt — remain fully in scope and are not de-prioritized; they simply do not carry the same calendar weight as the GCC six. Sierra Leone remains the African anchor with its own dedicated cadence (see below); the GCC priority and the Sierra Leone anchor are parallel rules, not competing ones.

## In scope — Africa (open list, anchored by Sierra Leone)

All African countries are in scope. **Sierra Leone is the anchor** — we should aim for regular coverage of Sierra Leone specifically, alongside coverage of other African nations.

## In scope — Asia (open list, with one exception)

All Asian countries are in scope **except Iran**. We have particular interest in:

- China
- Thailand
- Japan

But India, Vietnam, Indonesia, the Philippines, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Pakistan, the Central Asian republics, etc. are all welcome.

## In scope — Latin America and the Caribbean (open list)

All Latin American and Caribbean countries are in scope. No exclusions.

## Reduced focus — USA, Canada, Europe, Australia/NZ

Not banned, but the default is **don't publish**. Exceptions: a piece originating from these geographies may run *only if* the practice, policy, or story is genuinely transferable to in-scope geographies, **and** that transferability is the editorial frame. A piece on "what Finland teaches us" is only acceptable if it is really a piece about what an in-scope country could adopt.

## How to handle ambiguous cases

- **Diaspora communities:** A piece about, say, Lebanese educators in São Paulo is in scope (both Lebanon and Brazil are in scope).
- **Cross-border initiatives:** Fine if at least one anchor country is in scope.
- **International orgs (UNICEF, World Bank, OECD):** Fine as a source, but the piece must be about an in-scope geography, not about the institution.
- **Refugees and displacement:** Stories about education for refugees in in-scope countries (e.g., Syrian refugees in Jordan, Sudanese refugees in Sierra Leone) are explicitly welcome.

## The political question

This list reflects the founder's editorial conviction about which geographies deserve more international attention. Some inclusions and exclusions are politically loaded. The Editor agent does **not** debate the list. If a candidate piece touches a geopolitically sensitive context (e.g., West Bank schools, Syrian curriculum reform), the piece itself is reviewed for tone and sourcing rigor — but the *eligibility* of the country is not in question.
