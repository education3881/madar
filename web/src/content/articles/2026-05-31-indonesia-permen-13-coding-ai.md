On 22 July 2025 the Minister of Basic and Secondary Education, Abdul Mu'ti, sat in front of a webinar camera and addressed the country's teachers, principals, and curriculum officers. The occasion was the public socialisation of Regulation 13 of 2025 — *Permendikdasmen Nomor 13 Tahun 2025* — issued by his Ministry earlier that month. The Minister opened with a sentence that, in the rhythm of regulatory speech, is the kind of sentence that signals the listener should pay close attention to what is *not* being said.

"Saya tegaskan bahwa Permendikdasmen Nomor 13 Tahun 2025 yang telah terbit bukanlah tentang Kurikulum Baru."

*I emphasise that Regulation 13 of 2025, which has now been issued, is not about a New Curriculum.* The sentence is on the Ministry's press page; the recording is on the Ministry's YouTube channel. The emphasis — *I emphasise* — is unusual in a press communication that, on its face, is announcing administrative housekeeping. It is the kind of emphasis a government uses when it expects the announcement to be misread as something larger than it is.

The reason for the emphasis is that Indonesia has been through a decade of curriculum reform in living memory and the public is — to use a word the Minister himself avoided — exhausted by the cycle. The 2013 Curriculum (*Kurikulum 2013*, or K13) was the major Yudhoyono-era reform. The Independent Curriculum (*Kurikulum Merdeka*) was the major Jokowi-era reform under Minister Nadiem Makarim. When the Prabowo-Gibran cabinet took office in October 2024 and appointed Mu'ti to a new Ministry split off from the old Kementerian Pendidikan, Kebudayaan, Riset dan Teknologi, the natural expectation in the teacher corps was a third reform. Tempo reported in early November 2024 that the new Minister was *indicating a review* of the Merdeka Curriculum.

Eight months later, Regulation 13 of 2025 landed, and the Minister's first sentence about it was the disavowal. *Bukanlah tentang Kurikulum Baru.* The 2013 Curriculum and the Independent Curriculum both, the Minister told the webinar, "tetap dapat digunakan dan menjadi dasar pengelolaan pembelajaran di satuan pendidikan" — *remain usable, and the basis for the management of learning at education units.* The regulation, in the Minister's framing, was a *rangkaian terintegrasi* — an integrated series — combining Regulation 10 of 2025 (graduate competency standards) and Regulation 12 of 2025 (content standards) with a unifying pedagogical posture called *pembelajaran mendalam* — *deep learning.*

The Head of the Body for Standards, Curriculum and Educational Assessment, Toni Toharudin, who shared the webinar, used the same word — *penyempurnaan*, *refinement* — six times in the press release. "Penyempurnaan tersebut kami wujudkan melalui 8 dimensi profil lulusan, pendekatan pembelajaran mendalam, penerapan Koding dan Kecerdasan Artifisial sebagai mata pelajaran pilihan, kokurikuler yang lebih fleksibel, dan kehadiran pramuka..." *We have effected this refinement through eight dimensions of the graduate profile, the deep-learning approach, the introduction of Coding and Artificial Intelligence as elective subjects, more flexible co-curricular activity, and the presence of pramuka...* It is the third item on the list — *Koding dan Kecerdasan Artifisial* — that the rest of the country has been reading the regulation for.

> "AI and coding will be implemented as elective subjects starting this semester, in the 2025–2026 school year. The academic modules are ready, the learning objectives are set, and teacher training is already underway."

The quotation is from Minister Mu'ti, speaking on a visit to the Nusantara Capital Authority office in Sepaku, East Kalimantan, in August 2025. The Jakarta Globe's dispatch from that visit puts a number on the rollout: Coding and AI will be introduced in 59,000 elementary, junior high, and senior high schools across the country starting in the 2025–2026 academic year. The phased start is grades 5, 7 and 10 — that is, the entry years of the elementary, junior secondary and senior secondary stages. Two hours of class time per week in fifth-grade primary and junior high; up to five hours in senior high.

Fifty-nine thousand schools is not a pilot. It is, in scale, a national rollout dressed in pilot language. The dressing matters because of what the Minister was insisting on in the webinar: that this national rollout would not be experienced, by teachers or parents, as another curriculum-change cycle.

The dispatch from Sepaku makes the operational dressing visible. SDN 20 Sepaku Elementary School — a public primary school in the new national capital, located in the same district as the still-under-construction Nusantara presidential complex — has been equipped with a thirty-unit computer lab. The principal, Pujianto, told the visiting press: "We've started familiarizing students, especially those in Grades 5 and 6, with AI concepts. Some of our teachers already have the skills and are using them in class. We're rotating the computer lab use on a class-by-class basis, one per day, while we wait for specialized instructors to be assigned."

The architecture of that paragraph is what the rest of the rollout will sit on top of. Thirty computers, one class per day, no specialised teacher yet. Teachers who *already have the skills* doing what they can in the meantime. The Ministry has the academic framework written; it has begun training teachers; it has set the learning objectives. What it has not yet done at scale is field the specialised AI-and-coding teacher in each of those 59,000 schools. The arithmetic is heavy: if each school needs one trained subject teacher to deliver the elective at the grades-5/7/10 entry years, the country needs on the order of 59,000 newly trained or newly certified specialists in the field, beginning now. The Ministry has signalled a partnership with Google to train at least one million teachers in coding and AI, but the operational test will be how many of those trained teachers actually occupy the elective slot at the village school, and how many remain as the *math teacher who took the AI module*.

This is the place where the Minister's framing — *not a new curriculum, a refinement* — is doing operational work. A new curriculum, in the Indonesian school system, comes with new exams, new teacher-certification cycles, new textbook contracts, new headaches. A *refinement* of two existing curricula, with new electives layered into them, places no such obligation on the schools that have not yet reached the rollout phase. It also asks less of the public to absorb. The 2013 Curriculum and the Independent Curriculum continue to function. The eight dimensions of the *graduate profile* — a renaming of the Pancasila student-profile — are framed not as a replacement of the previous value architecture but as a "strengthening of competency standing within the Graduate Competency Standards." The kindergartener whose parent has heard about Permendikdasmen 13 of 2025 in the village WhatsApp group can be told, accurately, that *the curriculum has not changed*. Their child will, in five years, be offered Coding and AI as electives if the school has the teacher.

The Minister's closing sentence in the press release returns to the *deep learning* posture and to a register that is unusual for Indonesian regulatory communication. "Pembelajaran Koding dan Kecerdasan Artifisial adalah bagian dari upaya kita untuk merespons perkembangan teknologi sekaligus mewujudkan manusia Indonesia yang kritis, produktif, beretika, dan bertanggung jawab dalam mengembangkan dan memanfaatkan teknologi." *Coding and AI learning is part of our effort to respond to technological development while at the same time forming Indonesian persons who are critical, productive, ethical and responsible in developing and using technology.*

The closing word — *bertanggung jawab*, responsible — is the operational key to whether this rollout becomes the early-2030s thing the Ministry has wagered on. The infrastructure question (one trained teacher per school) is real; the rhetorical question (whether "refinement, not replacement" persuades a teacher corps that has seen three reforms in twelve years) is the larger one. If both go well, the Ministry will look back and say it slid coding and AI into the national curriculum without triggering reform fatigue, and a generation of Indonesian fifth-graders began learning to code in fifth grade because their school had a thirty-computer lab and a *math teacher who took the AI module*. If either fails, the country will have one more reform on the books that did not reach the village. The Ministry's first sentence — *I emphasise that this is not a new curriculum* — was the disavowal of that history. The next four years are when the disavowal is tested.
