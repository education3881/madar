In a second-year classroom in Ceará, a seven-year-old reads a short passage aloud, writes a sentence of her own, and is counted. The count now travels further than it once did. In June 2023 Brazil took a literacy model that a single state had spent nearly two decades building and made it a national compact — the Compromisso Nacional Criança Alfabetizada, instituted by federal decree and joined by every state, the Federal District, and almost every one of the country's 5,570 municipalities. To the compact it attached one annual number: the share of children reading and writing at the expected level by the end of the second year of primary school. That number has risen three years running — 56% in 2023, 59.2% in 2024, and 66% in 2025, announced in March 2026 — clearing the year's target of 64% and putting the 2030 goal of 80% within view.

The model is not new; only its reach is. For nearly twenty years the north-eastern state of Ceará has run a programme called *Alfabetização na Idade Certa* — literacy at the right age — born in the city of Sobral and built on a collaborative arrangement in which the state helps its municipalities get every child reading by seven. It is among the most-copied education programmes in the country, and it is what the federal compact scaled. Ceará has now taken the national literacy seal's Gold band two years in a row. "This result expresses the capillarity of our public policies," the state's education secretary, Eliana Estrela, said when the latest seals were announced, "and above all the alignment of purposes in guaranteeing that all Ceará children learn at the right age." Capillarity is the right word for what the compact is trying to reproduce: not a programme run from Brasília, but a habit pushed down into every municipal network that signs on.

The honest difficulty sits inside the headline number itself. The Indicador Criança Alfabetizada is designed by the federal assessment institute, INEP — which writes the test and fixes the bar at a cut score of 743 points, set by a 2023 study — but it is administered by the state school systems themselves, as a census of nearly two million second-years. Set beside the federal government's own external exam, the sample-based Saeb, the two measures disagree by design: for 2023 the state-run Indicador read 56% while the externally-run Saeb read 49.3%, a gap of seven points the education ministry has acknowledged in public, with the careful line that "the exam is not the indicator." Both use the same definition of a literate child; they differ in who holds the ruler. It would be easy, and wrong, to read that gap as the states marking their own homework. The Indicador is a census, comparable school-to-school and state-to-state; the Saeb is a sample whose margin of error, narrow nationally, runs past ten points inside many individual states — which is part of why the states built a census of their own. The number that grades itself is, in this case, the more comparable of the two. But it is still the number that grades itself, and a system that leads with it owes its readers the audited figure beside it. Brazil, to its credit, publishes both.

A national average also hides a continental spread. Sixty-six percent is the country; it is not the child. In the 2025 results, twenty of the twenty-seven federal units cleared the target — which is to say seven did not, and a state like Ceará sits far above the mean while others sit well below it. The instrument is built to expose exactly this, network by network, and the seals reward the networks that close the distance. But a reader who takes 66% as the experience of a typical Brazilian child has mistaken the mean for the distribution. The question the number really raises is not whether the average rose, but whether the floor did.

Two further limits belong on the record. The first is that the published indicator carries no public breakdown by race or family income, so it cannot yet say whether the gains are reaching the children furthest behind or are concentrated among those already near the bar — a caution the analysts at Todos Pela Educação have pressed. The second is that the instrument is still settling: INEP issued a 2025 rule expressly to strengthen the comparability of the second-year measurement across states, which means some part of a rising line reflects a ruler being filed true, not only children reading better. And the decree's literal goal — every child literate by the end of year two — sits a long way above the measured 66%. That distance is not a failure of the number; it is the difference between an aspiration written into law and a country measured honestly against it.

What Brazil has done is rarer than a rising percentage. It took a soft national goal and made it a hard local one, by attaching a public, annual, comparable figure to it and tying federal recognition to that figure — the mechanism by which a sentence in a decree becomes a habit in a classroom in Sobral. Whether the line keeps climbing is not yet knowable, and the spread and the silences above are real. But the part worth keeping is the discipline, not the score: a number changes what schools do only when someone is made to own it, and the test of any such system is what it does at the seam where the grader and the graded are the same hand. Brazil's answer, so far, is to print the audit next to the boast. That is not the whole of accountability. It is the half most systems skip.

— *The Editors*
