The Plan Ceibal of Uruguay turned eighteen years old in early 2025. The anniversary did not get a fanfare. What it got, on 28 March 2025, was the launch of a regional laboratory called EduIA Lab, presented in Montevideo by the programme's current president, Fiorella Haim, with the country's president, Yamandú Orsi, in the room. The lab is funded by the World Bank, the Inter-American Development Bank, and the IDRC of Canada. Its mandate is regional: to develop AI tools for teacher professional development, for student learning, and for educational management — across Latin America, with Uruguay as the anchor.

This is the third major reframing of an institution that began as a one-laptop-per-child programme in 2007 and whose original brief was simply *device access and connectivity*. The first reframing — accomplished by roughly 2011, after the laptops had been distributed and the Wi-Fi switched on in every public school — was from *device delivery* to *teacher training*. The second, around 2019, was from *computational thinking* to *computational thinking and artificial intelligence*. The third, made institutional in March 2025, is from *a national programme that quietly trains its own teachers in AI* into *a regional laboratory whose product is publishable, citable, and exportable AI-in-education practice*.

"Desde su origen, Ceibal tuvo una fuerte impronta de equidad" — from its origin, Ceibal had a strong stamp of equity — Haim said at the launch, recalling that the original mandate was about closing the access gap. "Estamos comprometidos a aportar nuestra experiencia y capacidad para acompañar al sistema educativo en la resolución de sus desafíos." *We are committed to bringing our experience and our capacity to accompany the educational system in resolving its challenges.* The framing is operational: experience is the input; the system's challenges are the output. The institution that holds the experience is offering it back.

The arithmetic that sits behind that offer is the part of the Ceibal story that has compounded most quietly over eighteen years. Plan Ceibal currently publishes more than three hundred formative offerings per year — courses, workshops, learning paths — that an average of ten thousand teachers per year take. The Pensamiento Computacional programme, renamed in 2024 to *Pensamiento Computacional e Inteligencia Artificial*, has passed two hundred and five thousand unique students through its curriculum and more than ten thousand teachers, across 85 per cent of the country's urban public schools. Uruguay has a primary-and-secondary public-school teaching corps of roughly fifty thousand. The implication of the second figure, then, is that one in five Uruguayan public-school teachers has taken a Ceibal-run AI or computational-thinking module at some point in the last five years.

This compounds. A teacher who took a *Pensamiento Computacional* module in 2021 and is teaching fifth grade in 2026 has had five years to integrate what she learned into her classroom practice; her replacement, when she retires, will inherit a school that has run AI-in-classroom workshops for nineteen years. The institution that delivered her training is the same institution — under the same name, with continuity of mandate — that delivered her colleagues' training in 2019, and her predecessors' training in 2014, and the original laptops to her school's storeroom in 2009. Few national education programmes in any country have maintained that continuity. The cost of *not* having maintained it is invisible in the success cases.

> "Hacemos historia, haciendo futuro."

President Orsi's launch-day phrase is, in Spanish, more elegant than its literal English (*we make history by making the future*). What it points to is the public-record argument Orsi was choosing to make at the launch: that Plan Ceibal has *trascendido gobiernos* — transcended administrations — and that this transcendence is not accidental. Uruguay has cycled through four presidents and three political parties since 2007. Plan Ceibal was launched under the Frente Amplio's Tabaré Vázquez. It expanded under his successor José Mujica. It survived the centre-right presidency of Luis Lacalle Pou, during which Leandro Folgar — Folgar's name is on most of the public-record AI-related quotes from 2022 to 2024 — held the presidency of the institution. Yamandú Orsi, the new Frente Amplio president in office from March 2025, appointed Haim. Through all four administrations the budget continued, the schools were maintained, the teacher courses were delivered. The institution survived, in other words, the largest predictor of institutional death in Latin America: a change of government.

What did the *institution that survived* look like by the time the EduIA Lab launched? On the public-record numbers: roughly three million devices distributed since 2007, 100 per cent Wi-Fi coverage in educational institutions, a continuous teacher-training pipeline through *Campus del Sur* (the Summer School), a state-owned platform called *CREA* that handles the digital-classroom layer, and a five-year-old AI-and-computational-thinking strand whose 80,000-student training milestone was already met in 2023. The 22,300 educators and students reported as participating in the 2025 Summer School — *Campus del Sur* — is, in scale, larger than the entire teacher corps of many Latin American countries.

Haim's framing of the EduIA Lab's specific job was, characteristically, modest in tone and ambitious in implication: "Sabemos que la apuesta que estamos haciendo es grande; por eso, buscamos generar alianzas más profundas y enfocadas." *We know that the bet we are making is large; for that reason, we seek to generate deeper and more focused alliances.* The institution that has compounded for eighteen years is signalling, in the launch-day language, that it does not want to do the *next* phase alone. The reason it does not want to do it alone is that the next phase — AI in education at curriculum scale — is the place where the cost of getting it wrong is no longer absorbable in one country.

The deeper observation, on Uruguay's posture toward the next decade, sits underneath the EduIA Lab's funder list. World Bank, IDB, IDRC-Canada — three institutions whose interest in education is fundamentally an interest in pedagogical evidence at scale. Uruguay is, in this configuration, neither a recipient country in the development-aid sense (its per-capita GDP is not where that argument lives) nor a vendor (the Lab does not sell its outputs). It is being asked, by three multilaterals, to be the laboratory in which the question *what does evidence-based AI-in-education actually look like at a national level* can be answered, with the resulting evidence published for the rest of the region. This is a particular kind of soft power, and it is being earned on the strength of nineteen years of compounding institutional discipline rather than on the strength of any single year's announcement.

For the schools themselves, the EduIA Lab's existence will not change very much in the short term. A fifth-grade teacher in a small school outside Salto will continue, in 2027, to teach the *Pensamiento Computacional e Inteligencia Artificial* module she took her certification course in. Her ninth-grade colleague will continue to take *Campus del Sur* in the January summer. The new students will continue to receive their devices. The change the Lab introduces is one layer above the classroom: the question of *which* AI tools the system places in which teacher's hands, and the evidence base from which that decision is made, is being moved from informal Ministry choice to formally researched institutional product.

The open question is the same open question that has run underneath Plan Ceibal since 2007 and has not yet been answered with finality. Whether a national institution that compounds quietly across two decades, training its teachers continuously in whatever the next-step technology turns out to be, produces a better-educated generation than one that runs higher-ceiling reforms at less institutional persistence. The EduIA Lab is a bet that the answer is *yes* and that the rest of Latin America will, in the next decade, find it worth importing. The 2027 anniversary — twenty years of Plan Ceibal — will arrive in the middle of the bet. The first publishable evidence from the Lab is expected within the next twelve to eighteen months. What it shows will matter not because it is dramatic, but because it has been earned at the slow pace of an institution that has not asked for drama in nineteen years.
