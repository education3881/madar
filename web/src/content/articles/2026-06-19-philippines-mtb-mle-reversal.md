A child starting school in the Philippines is, by the numbers, likely to arrive speaking a language her teacher may soon be told not to teach in. The country counts well over a hundred living languages, and more than nine in ten of its students speak, at home, something other than the Filipino or English in which the nation tests them. For a little over a decade, the law met that fact directly. Since the Enhanced Basic Education Act of 2013, public schools were required to teach the youngest grades — kindergarten through Grade 3 — in the child's mother tongue, on the well-supported theory that children learn to read fastest in a language they already understand. As of the school year now under way, that requirement is gone.

The change came in stages, each a matter of public record. The 2013 Act had made mother-tongue-based multilingual education — MTB-MLE, in the country's shorthand — a mandate. In August 2023, the department's new MATATAG curriculum dropped the mother tongue as a stand-alone subject. Then, on the 10th of October 2024, Republic Act 12027 lapsed into law — passing without the President's signature — discontinuing the mother tongue as the *medium* of instruction from kindergarten to Grade 3, restoring Filipino and English as the default languages, and permitting a child's first language only as an option in "monolingual" classes that meet four conditions: an established orthography, a grammar reference, a body of written literature, and teachers trained to use it. A department order, No. 20 of 2025, signed by Education Secretary Sonny Angara, made the new arrangement operational for the 2025–2026 school year. The same law instructs the department to review the optional programme every three years.

This publication has spent recent weeks at the same gap, approached from the other side. We reported on Iqra, a structured-reading programme in Ras al-Khaimah that a randomised trial found modestly but genuinely effective, and on Egypt's national effort to bridge the distance between the Arabic children speak and the Arabic they must read — a bridge, in that framing, built *from* the home language rather than against it. The Philippines is the same question with the sign reversed. Here the home language was, by law, the bridge; and the state that had legislated it has now legislated much of it away. What makes the case worth reporting is not that a country changed its mind — Korea reversed a flagship classroom policy on this masthead only days ago — but the grounds on which it did, and the uncomfortable fact that the evidence everyone invokes cannot, by itself, settle the argument.

That evidence usually arrives as a single dismal number. On the 2022 round of the OECD's PISA assessment, Filipino fifteen-year-olds scored 347 in reading against an average of 476, with only about a quarter reaching the level the OECD calls baseline proficiency. Both camps reach for that figure, and here the discipline matters: PISA tests fifteen-year-olds, in Filipino and English, years after the mother-tongue grades have ended. It can register that Philippine reading is in crisis; it cannot, by its own design, show that the mother-tongue policy *caused* the crisis, or that ending it will cure it. The same caution governs the World Bank's much-cited estimate of "learning poverty" — the share of ten-year-olds who cannot read and understand a simple passage — which stood near seven in ten before the pandemic and rose to roughly nine in ten after it. That is a dated baseline of the problem, sharpened by school closures, not a verdict on any one response to it. A number can be entirely real and still be the wrong instrument for the question being asked of it.

Strip away the misused statistics and a genuine dispute remains. The government's case, argued through its own review bodies — including the second Congressional Commission on Education, EDCOM 2 — is not that mother-tongue teaching is wrong in theory but that it failed in practice: too few trained teachers, too little material in languages that in some cases lacked even a settled spelling, and classrooms so linguistically mixed that "the mother tongue" was a fiction no single lesson could honour. The other side — which includes linguists who helped design the original policy, among them Ricardo Ma. Nolasco, a former chair of the national language commission, and the country's large teachers' unions — does not deny those failures. It argues that the answer to a policy starved of materials and training is to fund it, not end it, and warns that the reversal falls hardest on exactly the indigenous and rural children whose languages were least likely to qualify for the "optional" exception. Registering the change in late 2024, teachers' groups allowed that implementation had been hard while insisting that "outright discontinuation should not be the course of action."

Two things can be held at once, and an honest reading requires both. The first is that a mandate without the means to deliver it is its own kind of failure: a right to learn in one's own language is hollow if the books, the training and the teachers in that language never arrive, and the Philippines is not wrong to notice the distance between what its law promised and what its classrooms received. The second is that closing that distance by lowering the promise is a particular choice — and, by the law's own terms, a reviewable one. The question the country has set itself is not whether children should read; it is whether a system mends a bridge it cannot afford by repairing it or by routing around it. The three-year reviews the statute mandates are where that answer will appear — but only if the Philippines measures the children it has just moved, rather than re-reading, years too late, the scores of the ones it moved before.

— *The Editors*
