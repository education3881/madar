When South Korea's school year opened in March 2025, the country did something no system its size had tried: it put artificial intelligence into the official textbook. Not as an app a teacher might choose, but as the legally designated material for English and mathematics in the third and fourth grades, and for English, mathematics and computer science in the early years of middle and high school. The AI Digital Textbook — AIDT — had been approved by the Ministry of Education the previous September, a flagship of President Yoon Suk Yeol's government and of his education minister, Lee Ju-ho, its principal architect — and, in the ministry's own framing, the instrument of "personalised education for all." Five months later, on 4 August 2025, the National Assembly passed an amendment that rewrote the definition of a textbook to leave the thing out. AIDT did not enter the classroom and fail there in the ordinary way. It was, by act of parliament, reclassified out of the category it had been built to occupy.

It is worth being exact about what the law did, because the precision is the story. The amendment did not ban AI in Korean schools, and it did not order a single tablet switched off. It narrowed the statutory meaning of "textbook" to printed books and e-books, excluding "learning support software using intelligent information technology" — and in doing so demoted AIDT from official textbook to optional "educational material." The distinction is administrative and it is everything: a textbook is something schools are equipped and funded to adopt; educational material is something they may use if they wish. Overnight, a national mandate became a local option. The pilot it applied to was real but bounded — English and maths in grades three and four, English, maths and informatics in the first year of middle and high school — and the titles had been vetted; what changed was not their content but their standing.

The clearest account of why comes not from the law's opponents but from a teacher who used it. Kim Cha-myung, who teaches at an elementary school in Gwangmyeong, near Seoul, found the software genuinely useful: it saved him time and helped him reach pupils who were struggling. He still judged the program a failure, and was specific about the reason. "Everything was rushed," he told *Rest of World*; "it should have been rolled out gradually after testing its effectiveness." A high-school mathematics teacher, Lee Hyun-joon, described the daily texture of the problem — monitoring each student's progress through the system was harder than promised, and the overall quality, in his telling, was poor. These are not the objections of people who distrust the machine. They are the objections of practitioners who tried the machine and concluded the rollout had outrun the testing. That is a sharper indictment than refusal, and a more useful one, because it is falsifiable: it names what went wrong and when.

The numbers trace the same arc, and they need their scopes kept straight. Of South Korea's 11,932 schools, about 3,870 — roughly 32 percent — adopted AIDT as official material at the start, by a count compiled in the National Assembly. Adoption then fell rather than rose: from about 37 percent of eligible schools in the first semester, which ran to July, to about 19 percent in the semester that began in September; by mid-October, more than half of the schools that had signed on had dropped it. The money attached to the program sat in two separate streams that should never be added together — a 2024 project allocation of ₩533.3 billion, near $385 million, and a distinct three-year commitment of about ₩1 trillion, near $736 million, for teacher training and infrastructure across 2024 to 2026, as the World Bank recorded it. The publishers who had built the titles — among them Chunjae Education and YBM — filed an administrative suit against the Ministry of Education in April 2025, citing heavy losses in capital and staff. A flagship had become a liability claim.

None of which makes this a tidy story, and there are two endings the honest reading has to resist. The first is partisan. An identical bill to strip AIDT of its textbook status had been vetoed in 2024 by the acting president, Choi Sang-mok; the amendment passed in 2025 only after Yoon was removed from office and Lee Jae-myung, who had campaigned to rescind the policy, won the presidency. A reversal that tracks an election that closely invites the reading that a new government was simply undoing an old one's work. But the teachers who condemned the rollout were not running for anything, and Kim Cha-myung's complaint — that a useful tool was shipped before it was tested — is procedural, not political; it would be true under any government. The second ending to resist is the technological one: that AI had simply been found wanting in the classroom. Two facts cut against it. The reversal is not even legally settled — the publishers followed their damages suit with a constitutional complaint in December 2025, and as of the middle of 2026 the Constitutional Court has not ruled. And the same Korean system in which the mandate collapsed is the one the World Bank had held up, a year earlier, as a place where teachers were "leading an AI revolution" — voluntarily, in classrooms they chose. The tool schools were ordered to treat as a textbook was rejected; the tools teachers reached for on their own were not. The variable that moved was not the technology. It was the mandate.

That is the part worth carrying out of Korea, because other systems are making the same wager with different stakes. Uruguay built a public laboratory for AI in education and let schools opt in; Indonesia wrote coding and AI into the curriculum as an elective; South Korea designated AI a textbook and made it compulsory by that designation — and then had to legislate its way back out. The clocks are the difference. Making a tool mandatory is a fast act, available to any government with a majority; earning a classroom's trust in it is a slow one, available to no government by decree. Korea ran the first clock and skipped the second, and when the two came apart, the law could withdraw the mandate but could not retroactively supply the trust. The reversal is not a verdict on whether AI belongs in schools. It is a receipt for the order in which the two clocks have to be run.
