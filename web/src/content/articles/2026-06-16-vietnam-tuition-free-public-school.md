In a country of some 23 million schoolchildren, a sentence that sounds simple took two separate decisions to make true. On 28 February 2025 the Politburo of Vietnam's Communist Party resolved to waive tuition for every pupil in a public school, from preschool through the end of upper-secondary, beginning with the 2025–2026 school year. Four months later, on 26 June, the National Assembly turned the decision into law: Resolution 217/2025/QH15, passed by 440 of 441 deputies. The two instruments are not the same act, and a careful account keeps them apart — a Party decision sets the direction; a legislature's resolution, certified by National Assembly Chairman Trần Thanh Mẫn, is what binds a budget. Together they retired a fee that families across the country had paid, in some form, for generations. The headlines wrote themselves: school is free now.

It is worth being exact about what was made free, because the precision is the story. Tuition in Vietnam — *học phí* — is a monthly fee, capped and set by each province's People's Council within central limits. It is small. The Government Portal puts preschool tuition at between 50,000 and 540,000 dong a month and upper-secondary at between 50,000 and 650,000, depending on the district; at the mid-June 2026 exchange rate of about 26,400 dong to the US dollar, that is roughly two to twenty-five dollars a month. And much of the system was already exempt: primary tuition had been free under prior law, and five-year-olds were exempted from September 2024 under Decree 81. The genuinely new coverage in 2025 is therefore narrower than "free school" suggests — principally lower- and upper-secondary pupils, younger preschoolers, and a support track for families in private schools, who now receive a state subsidy capped at the public rate and pay the difference themselves. A real reform, reaching tens of millions. Also a reform aimed at one of the smaller lines in a household's education budget.

What the larger lines look like, a principal in Hanoi can describe better than any figure. Nguyễn Thị Vân Hồng leads Chương Dương Secondary School in Hoàn Kiếm district, and she told the newspaper *Tiền Phong*, in March 2025, what the exemption changes inside one building. About a tenth of her pupils come from families in real financial difficulty; a smaller group — by her account around one percent, eight to ten children — simply could not pay the tuition at all. They had stayed enrolled because her teachers had been quietly covering it, contributing something on the order of 155,000 dong a month per child, about six dollars, out of their own salaries. With tuition gone, she said, those families "no longer feel indebted to their teachers." It is the kind of detail that does two things at once: it shows the policy doing exactly the dignifying work its supporters claim, and it reveals that the fee had been small enough for a teacher's paycheque to absorb — which tells you something about where the real weight of schooling sits.

It does not sit on tuition. The most useful map of what remains is the one Tuoi Tre's Mỹ Dung drew in March 2025, itemising the costs a family still pays after the exemption. Health insurance is compulsory; the state subsidises part of the premium for some groups, but for most families it is a required out-of-pocket line. Then come the school-service fees set building by building: semi-boarding supervision, breakfast and meals, after-hours care, the salaried caregivers — in Ho Chi Minh City's 2024–25 schedule, up to 260,000 dong a month for the youngest groups. Then uniforms, textbooks, supplies, transport, and the after-school classes — *học thêm* — that a competitive system makes feel less than optional. And then the recurring "parents' fund," which the law says must be voluntary and which is, by years of domestic reporting, often not. Radio Free Asia, surveying the region in March 2025, quoted a Hanoi parent spending one to two million dong a month on tuition, meals and water, with a separate after-school fee on top — against an average national income, by the General Statistics Office, of about 7.7 million dong a month, roughly 290 dollars. Tuition was never the barrier that kept a low-income child at home. These are.

None of which makes the reform small. Its annual cost to the state is real — about 30,600 billion dong, near 1.2 billion dollars, a figure the Minister of Education and Training, Nguyễn Kim Sơn, has cited in defending it and in proposing the extension that brought private-school families into the support scheme. A government that chooses to spend at that scale on lowering the price of school, rather than on something more visible, is making a statement of priority, and the United Nations read it as one: UNICEF's representative in Vietnam, Silvia Danailov, called the exemption "an important step toward inclusion and equal opportunity for all children." She paired the praise with a caution of her own — that the exemption be matched by attention to the costs families still carry, "such as textbooks, uniforms, and transport." The equity instinct is sound, and even its champions know where it stops. The question is whether the instrument matches the instinct — whether waiving the cheapest fee reaches the child whom cost actually excludes.

That question is not a Vietnamese failing; it is the recurring shape of "free education" everywhere, and Vietnam's own press has been least romantic about it. The word for the problem already exists in the language — *lạm thu*, over-collection — and Vietnamese outlets have documented for years the way fees the state does not charge reappear, building by building, as fees a school does. The honest reading of 2025, then, is neither the press-release version nor the cynic's. The state lifted a charge that was small but not nothing, ended a quiet indebtedness in classrooms like Nguyễn Thị Vân Hồng's, and put more than a billion dollars a year behind the principle that public school should not bill its pupils. It also left standing the meals, the insurance, the uniforms, the after-school classes and the "voluntary" fund — the costs that actually decide whether a family on 7.7 million dong a month sends every child, every day. Tuition-free is a true description of the new law. Fee-free is not, and the distance between the two is exactly the distance a reform like this still has to travel.

There is a version of this story for almost every system that has announced free schooling and discovered that announcing it is the easy part. What makes Vietnam's worth watching is that the gap is documented in the country's own newspapers, in its own vocabulary, by reporters and principals willing to be specific about a popular policy's limits while it is still being celebrated. That is the harder kind of honesty, and the more useful one — because the second reform, the one that reaches the costs tuition never was, can only be built on an accurate account of the first.
