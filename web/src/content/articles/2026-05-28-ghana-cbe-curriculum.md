A child between the ages of eight and sixteen, who has either never been to school or who fell out of school early, sits in a small classroom somewhere in Ghana — in the Ashanti region in the south, or in the Upper West where Dagaare is spoken, or in the Volta region where Ewe is the language of the home. The classroom is part of Ghana's Complementary Basic Education programme. Over the next nine to twelve months, divided into three phases of sixty days each, three hours a day, the child will be taught lessons that build, week by week, the foundational reading and numeracy of the first three years of the national basic-school curriculum. On the fifth day of each week, the lesson is assessment and review. By the end of the programme, the goal is to transition into mainstream Ghanaian primary school — several years behind the cohort age, but functionally on the literacy and numeracy curve.

The classroom has run, in various forms, since 1995. The materials in the child's workbook are new.

On the 31st of March 2026, in Accra, Ghana's Complementary Education Agency launched the revised Complementary Basic Education curriculum that had been seven years in the rebuilding. The materials had been developed by a Technical Working Group convened by the Agency together with the National Council for Curriculum and Assessment, with technical input from the Luminos Fund, and with financing from UNICEF and the United Kingdom's Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office. Three thousand eight hundred and forty-four pages of new teaching and learning materials. Four Ghanaian languages — Dagaari, Dagbani, Asante Twi, and Ewe — out of a stated target of fifteen.

"This revised curriculum is not just a document, it is the outcome of shared commitment, technical excellence, and a common vision for the Ghanaian child," Dr. Clement Abas Apaak, Ghana's Deputy Minister of Education, said at the Accra launch, in remarks recorded by the Ghana News Agency. "Our priorities are to expand access, to improve quality, and to ensure meaningful learning outcomes for every Ghanaian child."

The Deputy Minister's framing is the framing a deputy minister speaks at a curriculum launch. The more interesting framing, in the same report, came from Mr. Daniel Kwesi Ashiamah, the Executive Director of the Complementary Education Agency itself. "We are creating pathways for their successful transition into mainstream education and lifelong learning," Mr. Ashiamah said of the children — out-of-school learners between eight and sixteen who, in 2026 Ghana, do not include the cohort the national education system is otherwise designed for. A few sentences later: "If the future of Ghana is non-negotiable, then the same applies to the quality education of the Ghanaian child and, more so, the out-of-school children."

The reason this particular launch is interesting — interesting in the slow institutional sense, not the press-release sense — is that the gap it is closing is, on paper, an unglamorous one. In 2019, Ghana introduced a new national curriculum for the formal basic-education system, updating learning benchmarks and teaching approaches from kindergarten through the first year of senior high school. The CBE curriculum, designed in the 1990s and meant to feed children back into mainstream school, was not updated alongside it. For seven academic years, the children completing the CBE programme were arriving in formal school equipped with foundational skills calibrated against a curriculum that no longer existed.

This is the structural mismatch the revised CBE curriculum is built to fix. The new materials are aligned with what the Luminos Fund, on the page describing the project, calls Ghana's Standard-Based Curriculum. The intervention is, on the face of it, editorial. The implication in classrooms is consequential: a child who completes CBE in 2027 in Dagbani will be reading and counting against the same benchmarks the in-school cohort is being tested against.

The mother-tongue commitment in the materials is doing more work than is usually visible from outside the curriculum-development conversation. Reading, in any language, is built on the sounds the language uses; a phonics progression that works in English does not work in Twi, and a progression that works in Twi does not work in Ewe. "These materials were not simply translated," the Luminos Fund's blog notes in its account of the project. "Expert teams developed materials to preserve a structured, phonics-based progression tailored to that language. Reading builds on sound patterns: a text that works in one language may not support learning in another." By designing each language version independently rather than translating from one base, the Agency is making the wager that early literacy in the language of the home, however much it complicates the production work, is the only foundation that holds.

The institutional scale numbers, supplied by external observers, give a sense of where the Agency is starting from. Since 2015, the CBE programme is reported to have reintegrated more than 500,000 out-of-school children into formal schooling. The Luminos Fund's own partnership with the Agency, dating to 2021, has touched some 12,000 children directly, in classrooms initially in the Ashanti region. The new materials, deployed across the four languages so far produced, are projected by Luminos to reach 13,000 children a year, with the figure climbing as the additional languages come online.

Osama Makkawi Khogali, the UNICEF Ghana representative, said at the launch: "We will continue to work until every child is in school, every child is learning and every child has the chance to reach their full potential." That is a generic UNICEF sentence, and its genericness is its strength. The genuinely difficult part of the work — what happens between a child who has been out of school for four or five years, a teacher in a CBE classroom in northern Ghana with new teacher-guide materials in Dagaare, and the inspector who in 2027 will check whether the child has, in fact, learned the things the curriculum says she should have learned — is the part neither press releases nor donor letters can fully cover.

The open question is whether the eleven languages still to come will get the same treatment. The Agency's stated target of fifteen is ambitious; producing 3,844 pages of culturally and linguistically specific phonics-based materials in four languages was, by the partners' own description, two years of work. Eleven more languages is, at the same pace, another five years. Whether that pace holds — and whether the curriculum the Ghanaian Ministry begins piloting in the 2025–26 school year reaches the Upper East, the Volta, and the Savannah on the same calendar — is the test the next school year is going to start running.
