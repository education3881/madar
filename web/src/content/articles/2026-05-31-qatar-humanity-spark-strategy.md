The Qatar National Convention Centre, on the afternoon of 2 September 2024, hosted the launch of a six-year education strategy under a one-line slogan. Igniting the Spark of Learning. The Prime Minister, Sheikh Mohammed bin Abdulrahman bin Jassim Al Thani, unveiled the document; the then-Minister of Education and Higher Education, Buthaina bint Ali Al Jabr Al Nuaimi, walked the audience through the four pillars. Two months later, on 12 November, the Minister was succeeded by Lolwah Al-Khater. The strategy, in other words, was conceived under one minister and inherited by another within its first quarter.

This is not unusual in any large administration; cabinet portfolios change. The unusual thing is the shape of the document itself, which sets out a series of operational commitments specific enough to be tested at the eighteen-month mark, and a rhetorical frame ambitious enough to be tested against the events of any given month. The operational commitments and the rhetorical frame have not yet been forced into the same room. They will be, soon, and the form that confrontation takes is what makes the strategy worth reading now.

The most specific commitment in the document, attributed to Minister Al Nuaimi at the launch, was a doubling target on early childhood education. "We aim to significantly boost early childhood education enrolment rates," she said. "Currently at 44%, we aim to double this figure by 2030." A doubling from 44 to 88 per cent in six years is a large operational ask in any school system; it requires either a substantial increase in public kindergarten capacity, or a private-sector enrolment mechanism the state can underwrite, or both. The strategy named both as instruments: "expanding the role of both public and private early childhood education institutions, coupled with initiatives to enhance their quality."

A second commitment, also from the launch, was the announcement of four new STEM schools to open by 2026, accommodating over 2,000 students collectively. That is roughly 500 students per school, a modest scale that suggests these are intended as flagship institutions seeding teaching practice rather than mass-enrolment facilities. A third, in the same press file, was a behavioural framework for the K-12 system: the *My School is My Community* programme, described as introducing "a new positive behavior policy and ethical charter while broadening the scope of student activities both domestically and internationally."

There is a fourth commitment that the launch document raises and then leaves open. The strategy, Minister Al Nuaimi said, was "designed to foster a culture of lifelong learning, equipping Qatari citizens with the skills and knowledge needed to thrive in a rapidly changing and highly competitive world." The phrase *rapidly changing* would, by November 2025, become the central operational question. Between September 2024 and November 2025, the public conversation about education in Qatar — and globally — was overtaken by the consequences of generative Artificial Intelligence at scale. The 2024-2030 strategy was written before that overtaking became unavoidable.

> "We are at a defining moment for education systems worldwide. As artificial intelligence and emerging technologies accelerate, it becomes vital to ensure that innovation amplifies human potential rather than replaces it."

The quotation is from Stavros N. Yiannouka, CEO of the World Innovation Summit for Education, a programme of Qatar Foundation founded in 2009. Yiannouka was speaking in the run-up to the twelfth biennial WISE summit, held in Doha on 24-25 November 2025 under the theme *Humanity.io: Human Values at the Heart of Education*. The summit gathered over two hundred speakers, sixty core sessions, and three thousand participants. The thematic tracks were five: putting human needs at the heart of education; realising the promise of progressive education; navigating the skills revolution; catalysing multi-sectoral systems change; and centering education towards economic opportunities and community resilience.

The choice of *Humanity.io* as the framing word for the summit is worth holding in the hand for a moment. It is a deliberate compound. *Io* is the standard suffix for tech-startup domain names, originating in the British Indian Ocean Territory's country-code TLD and adopted by the software industry as shorthand for *input/output*. The summit's framing places *humanity* in the slot a startup would place itself — proposing, rhetorically, that the question is whether the human is the input to the system or the system's intended output. It is a clean piece of language for a difficult question, and the question it makes available is the one the Qatar strategy will have to answer over the next four years: whether the AI capabilities the system equips its students with are arranged in service of the human, or whether the human is rearranged in service of the AI capabilities.

The summit's speaker list signalled the breadth of voices the Qatar Foundation invited into that question. The novelist Laila Lalami, whose work centres on identity and belonging in the Arabic-speaking diaspora. Mo Gawdat, the former Google X Chief Business Officer turned popular author on AI and well-being. The Nobel laureate Abhijit Banerjee, whose evidence base on poverty and education has reshaped what is considered measurable in the field. Anousheh Ansari of the XPRIZE Foundation. Omar Al Shogre, advocate for education access in Syrian conflict zones. Lady Mariéme Jamme of *iamtheCODE*, on digital literacy for girls across Africa. The summit was, in form, a curated argument about what the word *humanity* can do when it is asked to organise an education policy.

The implicit assertion underneath the summit, and underneath the strategy that hosts it, is that the *answer* to AI's pressure on schooling is not to retreat from AI, nor to flood schools with it, but to put the human values question first and let the technology choices follow. That is a coherent posture. It is also a posture that has to be defended against two harder ones: the pure-AI-readiness posture (treat AI as the curriculum), and the pure-traditionalist posture (treat AI as a threat to be contained at the school gate). Qatar is signalling, through both its strategy and its summit, that it will hold the middle position. Holding it operationally — across four thousand classrooms, four STEM schools, a doubling of ECE enrolment, and a minister handoff that happened before the ink was dry — is the work of the next four years.

The two figures that frame this period are Al Nuaimi and Al-Khater. Al Nuaimi, who launched the strategy and held the portfolio from October 2021 to November 2024, is the architect: her language at the launch — *catalyst*, *pillar*, *holistic*, *lifelong learning* — set the strategy's syntax. Al-Khater, who has held the portfolio since 12 November 2024, is the implementer; her record will be the test of whether the syntax can carry the operational weight Al Nuaimi described. The transition itself was untroubled in public record; the strategy was not paused, and the WISE 12 summit went forward under the new minister's tenure exactly as planned. But the consequence of a launch-minister handing off to an implementation-minister is that the institutional voice that defines what *success* means for the document is now plural rather than singular. That is, in Qatar's case, an asset rather than a vulnerability: two ministers in succession have endorsed the strategy publicly, and the second has presided over its largest cultural event without amending the frame. The strategy belongs to the state, not to its first author.

The open question is whether the *Humanity.io* frame survives contact with the operational year. The four STEM schools have to open by 2026. The ECE enrolment number has to begin moving toward 88 per cent. The teacher-development pipeline that *lifelong learning* implies has to start producing teachers who can hold the human-centred frame against the easier pressures — toward test-prep, toward AI-as-shortcut, toward measuring what is countable rather than what is taught. A six-year strategy that is rhetorically beautiful at launch and operationally vague at implementation is a category every education system has produced. The Qatar 2024-2030 strategy has not yet collapsed into that category, but it has not yet escaped it either. The eighteen-month mark — May 2026 — is roughly the date by which the first wave of operational evidence on the four STEM schools and the early childhood enrolment trend should be visible. We will read what is there to read when it is published.

For now, what is on the record is the document, the slogan, the summit, and the language. *Igniting the Spark of Learning.* *Humanity.io.* Two ministers, two phrases, one document. The hand that holds the match in 2026 is not the hand that struck it in 2024. Whether the flame is the same flame, or has been carried into a different room, is the question that the next operational year will answer.
