A second-floor library at Mosul University seats one thousand students when it is full. It was full, in the cautious-academic sense of the word "full" — a few visiting officials, the country's UN representative, a representative of the German government, a contingent of Mosul University staff — on the morning of Saturday the 19th of February 2022. The day was a long time coming.

Mosul University's Central Library was founded in 1921, the year the modern Iraqi state was, in different paperwork, founded. Over the following century it became one of the richest libraries in Iraq, second in volume only to the Central Library in Baghdad. By the early 2010s its collection numbered close to a million resources. During the occupation of Mosul by Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant between 2014 and 2017, the library building was bombarded by missiles. The United Nations Development Programme, which led the rehabilitation, estimates that between eight and ten thousand books and manuscripts were burned in the fire that followed. Some of what did not burn was looted; some was scattered.

The press release the UNDP issued on the day of the reopening is written in the language press releases of this kind are written in. The interesting paragraphs are the named-human ones, where the institutional voice gives way to a person speaking.

"The reopening of the Central Library and Ninewa Federal Court of Appeal is an important milestone in the journey to revitalizing this iconic city," said Zena Ali Ahmad, the UNDP Resident Representative in Iraq, on the day of the reopening. She continued, in the same statement: "I am proud that today, the number of students at the University has surpassed enrollment rates before ISIL occupation by over 40 percent. This shows the strength and resilience of the city — one that's well on its way to recovery after years of conflict."

The forty per cent figure is the part of Ms. Ahmad's statement that does the most work. A destroyed library can be made, depending on what one chooses to make of it, into evidence that a city is over, or evidence that it is not yet. The library that reopened in 2022, the same building rebuilt, with one thousand seats and the capacity for a hundred thousand books, sits in the same place it has sat since 1921. The fact that more students than before were registered at the University when the library reopened is not, by itself, a sentence about books. It is a sentence about whether the institutional reason for a library — a population of students who need one — was still present in the city after the war.

The financing chain is worth tracing, because the institutional reason and the institutional means are different problems. The rehabilitation of the Central Library was funded by the Federal Republic of Germany, channelled through the KfW Development Bank, with furniture provided by the Netherlands. The neighbouring Ninewa Federal Court of Appeal, reopened on the same day, was also funded by Germany; its furniture came from Australia. "We would not be here today if not for the support of one of our longest-standing partners, the Federal Republic of Germany," Ms. Ahmad said. "Together with Germany, we have made tremendous contributions to stabilization in Iraq."

The German side, in the same statement, was unusually direct. "Speaking to people in Mosul I understand how much it means to them that two central places of community life have risen like a phoenix from the ashes," said Peter Felten, the Chargé d'Affaires at the German Embassy in Baghdad. "Germany is pleased to have contributed to the rehabilitation of Mosul University's Central Library because we want the youth in Iraq to be able to build up the knowledge and skills needed to shape Iraq as a stable, sovereign, united, democratic and inclusive country."

Dr. Anna-Christine Janke, the Country Director of the KfW Office Iraq, spoke in a register that was characteristically more constrained, but the constraint is itself part of the story: "It is a great pleasure to witness the reopening of this facility and we look forward to seeing this important centre of learning used for exchange and interaction enriching the cultural and academic life at the University." That is what a development bank says when a development bank says the right thing.

That was 2022. The library had reopened. The next phase, less photogenic but arguably more important, began in 2024.

Under the UNESCO programme "Revive the Spirit of Mosul," which has been the institutional umbrella for the city's cultural-infrastructure rebuilding since 2018, a separate sub-project — "Restoring the Mosul University Library," funded by the Italian Agency for Development Cooperation — has been running from 2024 to 31 July 2025. Its task, on paper, is to make the library "fully functional": cataloguing systems, digital access tools, staff training. In October 2024, UNESCO, the University of Mosul, and the Iraqi Ministry of Higher Education and Scientific Research conducted a four-day visit to Ulster University in Northern Ireland for a Training of Trainers programme aimed at the library's staff. This is the slower work — the work that the press release on the reopening did not, and could not, cover.

A library is not finished when its building reopens. It is finished — in the sense in which a working library is ever finished — when the librarians know where the books are.

The open question is what the catalogue looks like at the end of July 2025, when the UNESCO sub-project closes out. Some of what the library lost — manuscripts that existed in single copies, the personal libraries of Mosuli scholars donated to the collection over the twentieth century — cannot be replaced, because they did not exist in duplicate. Some can be: institutional donations have arrived from universities and publishers across the world since 2017. The library that reopens its catalogue in 2025 will be a different library from the one that closed in 2014. Whether the city's students recognise it as the same institution — whether the forty per cent that came back in 2022 stays, builds, and uses it — is the question the next decade will answer.
