The Egyptian Knowledge Bank is, on paper, the kind of public infrastructure that a hundred international foundation reports would normally describe in the language of ambition and not the language of arithmetic. It launched on the 9th of January 2016, from a ceremony at the Cairo Opera House timed to Egyptian Youth Day. Full public access opened two weeks later. Anyone connecting to the internet from an Egyptian IP address, then and now, is granted free access to a content base that as of last year ran to roughly two hundred and fifty thousand e-books, one and a half million scientific theses, seven thousand international journals, and the catalogues of around fifty publishing houses, ranging from Nature to Britannica to Cambridge to Elsevier.

What is unusual about the EKB is not what it costs the user. It is what it costs the state, and what the state appears willing to keep paying.

The case for the project, in 2015, was made in the most direct terms a senior academic can manage in public. Dr. Tarek Shawki, then Dean of Sciences and Engineering at the American University in Cairo and chair of the Egyptian presidency's specialised council for education and scientific research, explained the negotiation he had spent the better part of two years closing. The publishers, he said in November of that year, would grant Egypt-wide free access to publications, journals, audio and video libraries, software, and books. The reasoning he gave for the project was about as plain as a sentence of this kind can be: *"By providing these materials free of charge, the knowledge bank ensures that all Egyptians, no matter what their economic circumstances, will have the tools they need to excel in their education and research."* The launch was framed, then, as a redistribution. A country had decided that a category of access — the kind that universities elsewhere fund through library budgets and individual researchers fund through paywalls — would be paid for federally, and used by anyone.

There is a separate, less-discussed face of the platform that comes in through a different URL. *study.ekb.eg* runs the K-12 catalogue, including curriculum-aligned video lessons, free e-books for schoolchildren, and the digital infrastructure that, during the COVID-era school closures, the Ministry of Education leaned on as the country's default emergency-learning channel. UNESCO's 2024 case study of the EKB, conducted under its Gateways Initiative, treats the K-12 layer as one of the platform's "five pillars" rather than as an afterthought; the K-12 register is, in the platform's self-account, central to what the EKB believes it is for. The international conversation about the EKB does not always remember this. The platform is, often, talked about as a research-access utility in a country with a strong research tradition. It is also a primary-school resource, taken seriously enough by its own designers to merit its own subdomain and its own catalogue logic.

Ten years on, the platform sits somewhere it would have been difficult to describe at the launch ceremony. Last year, the Ministry of Higher Education reported a fiscal-year readout that, taken plainly, is the sort of number-pile that says a thing has continued. One thousand one hundred and fifteen published scientific journals, five hundred and ten of them in Arabic. Two hundred and sixty-four million full-text downloads in a single year from the local-content side alone. A UNESCO Gateways designation in June 2025 naming the EKB as a globally leading platform of its kind. Whatever one thinks of any single one of those numbers, the directional fact is that the platform has not been quietly wound down between governments — and Egypt has had a different prime minister, a different higher-education minister, and several different politics in the years between 2016 and now. The platform has crossed administrative boundaries that were not promised at the founding.

The newer fact, and the harder fact to read, is this. From the 1st of June 2025, EKB began to ship — through the same portal, with the same login — Elsevier's generative-AI tools to its researcher cohort. Scopus AI, ScienceDirect AI, and the Reaxys Predictive Retrosynthesis Tool. These are not free tools. They sit, ordinarily, behind enterprise licensing of the kind that single research institutions in mid-sized countries cannot always afford. The EKB now provides them across the country. The Minister of Higher Education, Prof. Ayman Ashour, framed the integration in the Elsevier announcement in the register a minister tends to use: *"a leap forward for scientific advancement in Egypt"*, an expectation that AI tooling will *"inspire innovation, nurture homegrown talent, and help Egyptian researchers take leading roles on the global stage."* The Elsevier executive on the other side of the contract phrased it differently and, in its own way, more revealingly: the partnership is described as a *"deepening"* of a relationship that has been continuous since 2016.

The interesting question that the announcement itself does not raise is the one a reader of the document is, by paragraph three, ready to ask. When a state commissions, funds, and runs a national knowledge infrastructure for a decade, and then routes that infrastructure's next generation — its AI layer — through the proprietary stack of one foreign publisher, what has it committed itself to? The answer is not in the announcement. The answer will be in the next ten years of license renewals, training contracts, and the slow set of choices any large institution makes about which capabilities it builds in-house and which it rents.

A second question, which lives below the first, is the one Madār is more interested in. The K-12 platform — the *study.ekb.eg* side — has not, so far, been the public face of the AI integration. The June 2025 announcement does not mention the K-12 tier. The schoolchildren-and-teachers register of the platform is not, today, what gets the international press coverage. But it is, in the platform's own design, the larger user base. A platform whose research tier ships a Western publisher's AI tools, and whose primary-school tier does not, has, on quiet inspection, made an editorial decision about whose access matters most at this moment. That decision is worth a question the press release does not ask.

It is possible to read the EKB's ten-year arc as the most consequential publicly funded knowledge-access programme of its decade in any non-Western country. It is also possible to read it as a long-term lock-in, in which the cost of the access — paid in the currency of dependency on the same handful of publishers that the project was originally meant to make Egyptians less dependent on — comes due slowly, over years, and not at the signing ceremonies. Both readings sit on the same set of documents. The platform is, today, ten years into its life. The question of what it will look like at twenty is not one a publication of this kind should answer. It is one the publication should mark.
