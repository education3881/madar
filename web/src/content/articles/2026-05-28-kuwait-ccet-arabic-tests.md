A child of seven, eight, nine years old sits at a small table in a quiet room with a clinician. The clinician opens a workbook. The exercises she presents are not unusual: separate a spoken word into its sounds, hold a sequence of digits in memory and repeat them backward, read a list of words ordered by progressive difficulty. The test is recognisable to anyone who has ever encountered the assessment instruments used worldwide to identify dyslexia, dysgraphia, dyscalculia, and the related cluster of specific learning differences.

The unremarkable detail is that the test is in Arabic. The remarkable detail is that, for most of the twentieth century, it was not.

Up until the early 2000s, an Arabic-speaking child suspected of having a specific learning disability would, in most countries in the region, either be assessed using a translated English instrument — with all the noise that introduces into a phonological battery whose units of measurement are sounds the second language does not contain — or be assessed not at all. The Center for Child Evaluation and Teaching, a Kuwaiti charity registered in 1984, is the institution that, over the last forty-odd years, has assembled the alternative.

CCET's own self-description, on its English-language site, is matter-of-fact about the origin. "CCET was first established in 1984 in response to the needs of a group of Kuwaiti mothers who realised that their children were having difficulty with school work." A short paragraph farther down sets out the practical problem the mothers were trying to solve: the children's assessments had to be carried out abroad, in the United Kingdom and the United States, because no equivalent service existed in Kuwait. The mothers' first office, in the Shuweikh district near the sea front, was set up so that future children could be assessed without leaving the country. The institution was registered as a charity and qualified, on registration, for an annual grant from the Kuwaiti Ministry of Social Affairs and Labour.

This is the part of the story that the founding paragraph compresses into a sentence: a group of Kuwaiti mothers, headed by Mrs. Faten Al Bader, spent the first part of the 1980s building the institutional infrastructure that would, decades later, mean that a Kuwaiti child of seven could be assessed at home, in her first language, against a battery of tests standardised on Arabic-speaking children. The four central tests in that battery — the Test of Phonological Processing for Children, the Test of Orthographic Processing and Morphological Awareness for Children, the Test of Reading and Spelling for Children, and the Test of Working Memory for Children — were produced by the Centre over the course of a five-year research programme, working with children aged seven through eleven in Kuwait. They are nationally standardised. They were not translated from English. They were built.

The Centre's other long project, recorded by the International Dyslexia Association in its 2013 profile of CCET, is what the Centre calls the Multi-sensory Structured Arabic Language Programme. The Programme — a remediation curriculum in Arabic for children with reading disabilities, rather than an assessment tool — represents what Dr. Gad Elbeheri, who was at the time of writing a member of IDA's Board of Directors and who chaired its Global Partners Committee, described as a "one million dollar project that CCET has completed." Technical assistance on the Programme came from Professor Charles Haynes of Massachusetts General Hospital, a past IDA board member. The methodology followed Jeanne Chall's stages of reading acquisition and the principles of Multi-Sensory Language teaching that IDA has championed since the 1940s. The transposition into Arabic was the new work.

The collaboration between the Centre and the Kuwaiti Ministry of Education is the third leg of the structure. CCET runs both a morning programme and an evening programme — direct intervention with children, in English and Arabic — in formal cooperation with the Ministry. The Centre conducts, by its own count, around four hundred psycho-educational evaluations a year. Its public library, helpline, and lecture series operate at the awareness level; its assessment unit at the diagnostic level; and the morning and evening programmes at the remedial level. The institution covers, in other words, the full chain from a parent's first suspicion that something is different to the multi-year remediation programme the child eventually enters.

The thing the Centre is now in a position to do, that almost no other institution in the Arabic-speaking world can do, is *iterate*. A child assessed at CCET today, with the working-memory test the Centre standardised in the late 2000s, can be re-assessed in three years on the same instrument. The longitudinal record across Arabic-speaking children with specific learning disabilities — a category that for most of the twentieth century had to import its evidence base from English-language research — is being built quietly, one annual cycle at a time, in a small building in the Al Surra district of Kuwait City. The institution is not large. The library is open to the public. The helpline picks up.

The open question is the regional one. Arabic is the first language of children in roughly twenty-five countries. The CCET tests are standardised on Kuwaiti children; the dialect, the curriculum, and the sociolinguistic register vary across the Levant, the Maghreb, the Gulf, the Horn of Africa. The Multi-Sensory Structured Arabic Language Programme has been built once. The work of adapting and re-standardising it for, say, the colloquial Arabic of Casablanca — which is the work of years, not months — is the form the next phase of this institution's history will take, if it takes one. The question is whether the regional infrastructure to do that work exists, or whether, forty years after a small group of Kuwaiti mothers built it for their own children, the next set of children will have to wait for the next set of mothers to build it again.
