Hawa† keeps her chalk in a tin that once held condensed milk. There are seven pieces in the tin at the start of this school term, three of them already worn down to the length of a thumbnail. She teaches Primary 3 in a government school in Bo, the second city of Sierra Leone, and she has done so since 2017 — which is to say, since one year before the country's Free Quality School Education policy was announced, and through every revision of it since.

The policy turned ten in 2026. The ministry marked the anniversary with a circular. There were no celebrations in Hawa's school, because in the schools that the policy was supposedly for, the policy is not an anniversary; it is a working condition.

What the policy did is well documented and worth restating plainly. From 2018, the Government of Sierra Leone undertook to cover tuition, core textbook costs, and the public-examination fees that had until then sat between many children and the next grade. Enrolment rose. The 2023 Annual School Census, published the following year, counted roughly 2.9 million children in primary and junior-secondary classrooms, a figure that the ministry's own teams treat with appropriate caution — attendance and enrolment are not the same number — but which nobody seriously disputes as a directional fact. More children, in more places, walking through more gates.

What the policy did not do, and could not have done at its budget envelope, is correspondingly resource the teachers waiting on the other side of those gates. Hawa's monthly take-home, after the 2022 salary-scale revision by the Teaching Service Commission, sits at a figure that requires no editorial gloss: it does not cover the rent of a two-room house in Bo plus the school fees of her own two children at a different school across town. She does private tutoring on Saturdays. She buys her own chalk when the school's allocation runs out, which it does, every term, by around the seventh week.

This is the part of the Sierra Leone story that the international education conversation has trouble holding in one hand. The free-school policy is, by most reasonable measures, one of the more consequential domestic education reforms on the African continent in the last fifteen years. It is also, simultaneously, a policy whose success has been quietly subsidized by the people it most relied on, in the form of unpaid hours, personal expenditure on classroom materials, and a professional status that has not risen with the policy's profile. Both things are true. Neither cancels the other.

We mention this now, in this first issue of Madār, because we suspect the next decade of education writing about places like Sierra Leone will be tempted to talk about teachers in two registers, both of them wrong. The first register is the heroic — the Teacher Who Gives Everything, narrated in the warm tones of a fundraising appeal. The second is the technocratic — the Teacher As Variable, an input in a regression that explains learning outcomes. Hawa is neither of these. She is a worker with a profession, doing a job that her country has, in the last ten years, asked considerably more of, while paying her in real terms slightly less.

The cost of chalk is a small thing to write about. We are aware of this. But the small things are what survive the policy cycle. Ministries change; ministers change; the international financing partners rotate every three to five years; the framing language for what the country is trying to do shifts from "access" to "quality" to "learning outcomes" to whatever the next term of art will be. The piece of chalk in the milk tin does not change. Neither does the arithmetic by which a teacher in Bo, in 2026, calculates how many days of her own salary she will quietly spend on the public service that the state, in principle, provides for free.

There is a tendency, in this kind of paragraph, to gesture toward a recommendation. A call for a teacher-pay review. A line about how the next phase of the reform should focus on the workforce. We will not gesture. The Teaching Service Commission has issued credible recommendations on this point already, in documents that have been on the ministry's website since at least 2022. The argument is not a missing argument. What is missing is the political weight behind it, and political weight is not a thing an editorial publication based outside the country has any business pretending to supply.

What an editorial publication can do, and what we will try to do, is keep the chalk in the picture.

There is a Krio phrase Hawa used when we spoke with her, twice, by phone, in the first week of May. *Lan fɔ lan.* Learn to learn. It is what she tells her Primary 3 students when they finish a piece of work and she has nothing material left to give them — no sticker, no extra book, no card to send home. She tells them that the work itself is the reward, because the work is the thing that makes the next piece of work possible.

It is a beautiful sentence and we are aware of how easily, in a publication of this kind, a beautiful sentence from a teacher in a country the reader has perhaps never visited can be made to do sentimental work. We are trying not to do that here. The sentence is in this piece because Hawa said it, and because she said it in the specific context of explaining, without complaint, how she rations a tin of chalk across an eleven-week term.

The Free Quality School Education policy is a real achievement. The teacher in Bo who still uses last term's chalk is a real person. The publication you are reading begins, deliberately, with both of those facts at the same time, in the same paragraph, because we think the rest of what we publish over the coming year will only make sense if those are the terms on which we are starting.

— *The Editors*
