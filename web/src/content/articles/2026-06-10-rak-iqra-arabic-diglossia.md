There is a fact about Arabic that every teacher in the region knows and almost no school timetable acknowledges: no child speaks the language of the page. A five-year-old in Ras al-Khaimah (رأس الخيمة) arrives at school fluent — in Emirati Arabic, or Egyptian, or Levantine, whatever the kitchen speaks. The book in front of her is written in Modern Standard Arabic, the *fuṣḥā* of textbooks, news bulletins and Friday sermons. The two are relatives, not twins. Sounds differ, common words differ, the grammar that organises a sentence differs. Linguists call the arrangement diglossia, and its cost is paid at exactly the age when reading is supposed to begin.

The cost is measurable, and the research has stopped being polite about it. Elinor Saiegh-Haddad, whose 2025 paper in *Reading Research Quarterly* is the field's most direct recent statement, puts the spoken language first in every sense: a child's reading comprehension in Standard Arabic is built on the vernacular she actually possesses, and the distance between the two drags on every sub-skill that early reading is made of — hearing the sounds inside a word, mapping letters onto those sounds, retrieving what a written word means. The operational version of the same fact comes from Hanadi Mohammed, education and community development manager at the Sheikh Saud bin Saqr Al Qasimi Foundation for Policy Research in Ras al-Khaimah. "Across the Arab world, many children struggle with reading not because they lack ability, but because the Arabic they speak at home is different from the Modern Standard Arabic they are expected to read at school," she told The National in May 2026. By the end of Grade 3, she said, many students can comfortably read texts of only 200 to 400 words — leaving Arabic-speaking children "up to two full years behind their global peers."

What makes Ras al-Khaimah worth a column is not the diagnosis, which is decades old, but what the emirate did with it. The foundation built a program called Iqra — the Arabic imperative *read*, and not incidentally the first word of the Quranic revelation — designed with Helen Abadzi, the cognitive psychologist whose career argument is that early reading is a perceptual skill before it is a literary one. Iqra's wager is sequencing. "Traditional classrooms often introduce multiple literacy skills simultaneously and may place greater emphasis on memorisation, comprehension, grammar, or textbook coverage from an early stage," Mohammed said. "Iqra instead focuses very intentionally on foundational reading fluency and decoding skills first." In practice that means letters and sounds before grammar, and a classroom that sounds different: repeated oral reading, paired reading, echo reading of short texts, choral reading in unison — automaticity drilled the way a music teacher drills scales, so that written Arabic's patterns stop being foreign objects.

Then — and this is the part that earns the piece its place in this publication — the foundation let someone else mark its homework. During the 2024–25 academic year, J-PAL MENA, the regional office of the Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab, ran a randomised evaluation across 83 classrooms in 26 Ras al-Khaimah schools: 41 classrooms were assigned Iqra, 42 continued with the standard Arabic curriculum. The results, announced as the emirate confirmed a wider rollout this May, were unambiguous in direction and honest in size. Children in Iqra classrooms progressed in Arabic reading roughly 25 percent faster than their peers — about a quarter of a school year's extra learning, by the evaluation's measure. Overall literacy moved the median student from the 50th to the 54th percentile; word reading, the strongest result, from the 50th to the 58th. Gains held for native and non-native speakers alike, across letter identification, word reading, non-word decoding and oral reading fluency. "The evaluation shows that Iqra changed classroom practice and translated it into measurable improvements in student learning," said Nayera Adly Husseiny, the J-PAL MENA lead who oversaw the work — adding the sentence ministries most need pinned above their desks: "Pedagogy matters. How children are taught can make a significant difference."

Two details matter more than the headline numbers. First, in 21 of the 26 trial schools Iqra ran entirely inside the regular timetable, after only a few days of teacher training — no extra hours, no devices, no consultants in residence. Second, the emirate acted on the result: from September 2026, Iqra will run in Ras al-Khaimah's private schools from KG1 through Grade 1, alongside — not replacing — the Ministry of Education's Arabic curriculum, which since last year already mandates 40 minutes of daily Arabic for private-school kindergartners nationwide. "When Arabic is taught in a way that reflects how children actually learn to read, progress can be rapid, measurable, and achievable within existing school systems," said Dr Natasha Ridge, the foundation's executive director.

Now the inconvenient half, which sits in the same piece because that is where it belongs. The first inconvenience is the frame itself. A live current in the research — stated most directly in a 2025 *Frontiers in Education* paper advocating a "non-deficit model" of diglossia — objects to treating the child's spoken Arabic as a problem to be corrected. The vernacular is not broken *fuṣḥā*; it is a complete language the child has already mastered, and the honest pedagogy builds the bridge *from* it rather than bulldozing it. Iqra's results do not settle that dispute, and this piece does not pretend they do. The fairest reading is that the program succeeds precisely where it behaves like a bridge — structured, repeated exposure to written patterns a child's ear does not already own — and that any system tempted to read these results as a mandate to shame the playground dialect out of children has misread them. The second inconvenience is scale. Twenty-six schools in one small emirate is a trial; a four-percentile move in the median is real and modest; the rollout covers private schools, not the public system; and the gains were measured in June, not re-measured the following year. Whether a result earned in a Ras al-Khaimah classroom of 25 transfers to a Cairo classroom of 50 is exactly the question the program's earlier pilots in Egypt, Morocco and Jordan have yet to answer at scale. One more caution belongs in print, because the program's own history invites the error: an earlier Iqra study, published by the same foundation around 2020, carries different numbers from a different cohort. Every figure in this piece belongs to the 2024–25 J-PAL cycle, and to no other.

The mirror that makes this more than a Gulf story is Egypt, where over a hundred million people speak the world's largest Arabic vernacular, and where — as a March 2026 Egyptian Streets explainer laid out — the same diglossic gap shapes early literacy at a scale no emirate can model, without, so far, an equivalently evaluated national fix. The structural rhyme with our Sierra Leone coverage is hard to miss: a system that named an unglamorous mechanical problem, built an unglamorous mechanical fix, paid for an independent measurement, and published the modest truth instead of the press release. That sequence remains rarer in education than it has any right to be.

What Ras al-Khaimah has really demonstrated is not a reading method. It is a posture toward the question — that the distance between the Arabic of the home and the Arabic of the page is an engineering problem, addressable with sequencing and patience, rather than a deficiency in the child. The program's own name carries the oldest version of the instruction. The respectful reading of its results is the one its evidence actually supports: meet the child in the language she has.
