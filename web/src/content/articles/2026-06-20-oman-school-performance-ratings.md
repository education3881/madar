A parent in the Omani town of Seeb can now do something that, until this year, was not possible in the Sultanate and remains unusual across the Gulf: open a government website and read an official verdict on their child's school. In one case they will find it placed at the very bottom of a new national scale, in the band reserved for schools judged to fall short of the minimum standard and to need rapid intervention. The school is named. So is the standard it failed to meet.

The verdict comes from the National System for Evaluating School Performance, run by the Oman Authority for Academic Accreditation and Quality Assurance of Education — an external inspectorate built on the Sultanate's 2023 School Education Law and pointed at the goals of Oman Vision 2040. It was launched in December 2024 under the patronage of HH Sayyid Theyazin bin Haitham, the Minister of Culture, Sports and Youth; its first evaluation phase ran from September 2025; and in January 2026 the authority began posting per-school results to a public portal. The first phase reached 58 government and private schools, graded against a published scale that runs from "outstanding" at the top down to a bottom band for schools that fail to meet the minimum and need rapid intervention. One government school — al-Hassan bin Hashim, in Seeb — was placed in that lowest band.

Numbers like these are usually kept inside ministries. Across much of the region, school-inspection data, where it is gathered at all, informs officials rather than parents; the act of naming an individual public school as failing, in public, is rare enough that when this masthead reported Bahrain doing something comparable last month, it was the transparency and not the grades that made the story. Oman has now joined that short list — and pressed further than most, letting a citizen look up a particular school by name and read where the state has placed it.

The template for this kind of external, standards-based, published inspection is well known: it is England's Ofsted, the model much of the inspecting world has borrowed for three decades. Which makes the timing worth noticing, because in England the single public verdict has lately been in retreat. After a coroner found that an inspection had contributed to the death of a head teacher, Ruth Perry — whose school had been downgraded to the lowest available grade — the government scrapped one-word overall judgements in September 2024, replacing the single label with fuller "report cards" meant to show a school in more than one dimension. The objection was never to inspection itself but to the headline: that compressing a whole institution into a single public word can mislead the parent who reads it and break the teacher who receives it, and that a system powerful enough to do both owes the people it judges more than a word. Oman has embraced the headline at the moment its model is letting it go.

None of which settles whether Oman is right, and it is not this publication's place to hand down a grade of its own. A new inspectorate that publishes its findings is, on its face, an accountability a parent can only welcome; opacity has sheltered plenty of failing schools. The real question an inspection regime answers is not whether to judge but what follows the judgement. Oman's own framing is encouraging here — the authority says schools that score low will be notified and supported to improve — and the measure of the system will be whether that support, a route of appeal, and the fuller picture a single band cannot hold arrive as reliably as the grade does. A verdict that names without mending is only exposure; a verdict that names in order to mend is the entire point of inspecting. Oman has built the naming. Whether it has built the mending is what the coming phases will show.

Two honest limits sit on this account, and the story is the kind that is poorer for hiding them. The first is whose voice it carries: we have the authority's, including its chief executive, Dr Jokha bint Abdullah al Shukaili, but not yet the principals and teachers inside the named schools, whose account of an inspection is the part the public record, in any country, surfaces last — and, if the Ofsted reckoning is any guide, the part that matters most. The second is arithmetic. The authority's portal carries the per-school results, but the band-by-band tallies relayed second-hand in the press do not yet reconcile, so we have reported only what can be read directly — the first phase's reach, and the single school placed at the bottom of it — and left the disputed breakdown for the portal to settle. On a story whose whole merit is that a state chose to show its work, it would be a poor tribute to round the figures.

— *The Editors*
