Content · shipped — "The mother tongue, made optional" (EN + AR)
The Philippines counts 100+ languages, and more than nine in ten children speak, at home, something other than the Filipino or English they're tested in. For a decade the law required the youngest grades to be taught in the mother tongue; as of this school year, Republic Act 12027 (lapsed into law 10 Oct 2024) and DepEd Order 20, s. 2025 have ended that mandate, reverting to Filipino + English and leaving the home language optional only in qualifying monolingual classes. The piece refuses to take a side on a genuinely contested question and instead does the thing the wire stories don't: it shows that the failing score both camps cite — PISA 347 — cannot, by its own design, judge the policy (it tests 15-year-olds, in Filipino/English, years after the K–3 mother-tongue grades end). It's the contrast case that completes the language-of-instruction cluster (Iqra + Egypt build the bridge; the Philippines legislates it optional) and rhymes with the Korea statutory reversal.

Research-to-learn · ruling #10, the right-instrument figure
Today's spine became a durable rule so the next policy-dispute piece starts ahead: a correctly-read number can still be the wrong instrument for the claim it's drafted into. Before any figure carries causal or adjudicating weight, match it to the policy's population, timing, and mechanism (PISA fails all three against a K–3 home-language policy: 15-year-olds, post-grades, tested in the non-home languages).
