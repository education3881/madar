Content · shipped — "The gap the size of a country" (EN + AR)
Egypt's National Programme for Developing Arabic Language Skills — a sixty-hour remedial course — is the national-scale answer to the diglossia gap: every Arabic-speaking child must learn to read fuṣḥā, a form no one speaks at home. What earns the piece its place is not the programme but the measurement. UNICEF's Innocenti office embedded a quasi-experimental study and reported a defensible, modest result — 180,000 students, 980 schools, +17% literacy vs non-participants, single-letter illiteracy down from a third to under 2%. The piece sets that measured number against the much larger announced ones (half a million, then 1.3 million across 27 governorates) and refuses to add them — "a category error… it sums a number that survived an outside test with numbers that have not yet faced one." It closes the open question our 06-10 Iqra piece left in print (Egypt had "no equivalently evaluated national fix") and anchors the language-of-instruction cluster: Iqra ↔ Kuwait ↔ Egypt.

Research-to-learn · the measured-vs-announced rule, banked as ruling #9
Today's spine became a durable, transferable discipline so the next government-flagship piece starts ahead: when one programme reports its scale through several bodies (an independent evaluator, its own comms, the government), they are different currencies — lead with the measured one, label the rest "programme-reported," and never sum across the seam. Companion sub-rule: date recirculated baselines (Egypt's "six-in-ten cannot read" is pre-programme learning-poverty data, not a 2025 result). It's the third dimension of the existing "a program name is not a citation" rule.
