Content · the thirteenth piece, chosen and gated — Korea's AI textbooks, adoption to statutory reversal
The recon is banked; the drafting is not started; that order is the point. The arc: September 2024, Korea's education ministry approves 76 AI-driven textbook titles; March 2025, they enter classrooms; one semester of documented friction (inaccuracies, privacy, screen-time, heavier teacher workload — not lighter); 3 June 2025, a president elected having campaigned against them; 4 August 2025, the National Assembly narrows the legal definition of "textbook" to exclude them. The 2026 state is the story: adoption discretionary at ~30% of schools, early-complying schools defunded mid-subscription, publishers stranded. The piece is NOT "AI failed" — it is what a reversal costs, who pays it, and what it means that a democracy could execute one at all. The inconvenient half runs backwards here: the steelman of the program (process failure ≠ concept failure; Korean teachers were simultaneously leading voluntary AI adoption elsewhere in the same system). Two gates open before drafting, exactly as Iqra's were: a named operational voice (the Rest of World record names a parent organiser and a policy researcher, but not yet the person who ran it), and a figure trace — the spend figures disagree across press tiers ($850M vs ₩1T vs $580M, likely different scopes), which is itself a finding. Vietnam's tuition-free 2025–26 (23.2M students) is banked as the carried second candidate.
