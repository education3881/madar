# Refinement to ruling #12 — comparability is not independence (the census-vs-sample axis)

**Banked:** 2026-06-25 · **Owner:** Researcher / Editor · **Type:** durable ruling refinement
**Extends:** `2026-06-24-self-administered-vs-external-assessment.md` (ruling #12). **Paid for by:** the Brazil ICA ship (2026-06-25), live re-verification of the Indicador-vs-Saeb seam.

---

## The trap this closes

Ruling #12 (banked 06-24) ranked assessment figures by **administration independence**: self-administered < externally-administered < (the most independent reading wins trust). That ordering is right about *independence* — but it quietly smuggles in a second, false claim: that the **more independent** measure is therefore the **more trustworthy** one to compare across units. The Brazil draft, verified live, shows that is not always so — and a writer who treats "external = better number" will mis-attribute the gap.

## What Brazil actually showed

Two Brazilian literacy measures, same skill, same 743-point cutoff (set by the 2023 *Alfabetiza Brasil* study), same year:

| Measure | Administered by | Coverage | 2023 reading |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Indicador Criança Alfabetizada (ICA)** | the **state** school systems (self-administered) | **census** (~2 million 2nd-years) | **56%** |
| **Saeb** (federal external exam) | the **federal** government (external) | **sample** | **49.3%** |

The naïve ruling-#12 read: "trust the external 49.3%; the states are flattering themselves to 56%." **Wrong.** The reason the states built the ICA census is that the Saeb **sample's per-state margin of error runs past 10 points** inside many states (≈2.8 pts nationally) — so the Saeb *cannot* be compared state-to-state or municipality-to-municipality, which is exactly the comparison a federal-compact-with-local-seals needs. The **self-administered census is, here, the *more comparable* instrument.** Independence went one way; comparability went the other.

## The rule

**Administration independence and measurement comparability are two different axes. Tag a figure on BOTH, and name which axis explains a disagreement.**

- **Independence axis** (ruling #12): self-administered → externally-administered → independently-audited. Higher = harder to flatter.
- **Comparability axis** (this note): sample → census; un-moderated → moderated/standardised. Higher = safer to rank units against each other.

A measure can be high on one and low on the other. An **external sample** (Saeb) is independent but not unit-comparable. A **self-administered census** on a shared external cutoff (ICA) is unit-comparable but not independent. Neither is "the true number"; each answers a different question — *is the system grading itself honestly?* (independence) vs *which districts are ahead?* (comparability).

**Therefore:** when two figures for the same skill disagree, do not reflexively lead with the more independent one. State which question you are answering, attribute each figure to its administration **and** coverage, and **name the gap as the finding** (#12). If a system publishes both — as Brazil does — say so plainly; publishing the less flattering external number beside the headline is itself the credibility signal (the kin of ruling #11's "the disclosure is the signal").

## Verdict-checklist line (adds to the #12 line)
*Assessment figure: administration = self / external / audited ☐ · coverage = census / sample ☐ · if two disagree, which axis explains it, both printed & attributed ☐.*
