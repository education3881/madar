# Method — the measured-vs-announced rule, and dating recirculated baselines

**Banked:** 2026-06-18 (Egypt NPDA / Arabic-literacy ship) · **Owner:** Researcher / Editor · **Type:** durable sourcing discipline
**Family:** the third dimension of guidebook ruling #4 ("a program name is not a citation"). #4 covers *two studies, same name* (check the year) and *same figure, different scope* (fence each scope). This adds *same programme, different reporting body* (don't change currencies).

---

## The trap (Egypt NPDA, lived)

One programme reported its own scale through **three different bodies, at three different magnitudes**, all live in 2025–26:

| Reporting body | What it said | What kind of claim it is |
|---|---|---|
| **UNICEF Innocenti** (Sept 2025, embedded, quasi-experimental) | 180,000+ students · 980 schools · 10 governorates · 12 weeks · literacy **+17% vs non-participants** · single-letter-illiteracy ⅓ → <2% · 95% fidelity | **measured** — survived an independent, controlled test |
| **UNICEF Egypt comms** (feature, Mar 2026) | "half a million students across 1,000 schools" in phase 1 | **announced** — reach, self-reported |
| **Egyptian govt / PM-review** | ~1.38m students tested · ~30,000 teachers · all 27 governorates · weak-reader share 45.5% → 32.4% → 13.9% | **announced** — reach + self-assessment, by phase |

The error a hurried writer (or an eager ministry) makes: treat all three as the same programme's "results" and **sum or average them** — quoting "1.38 million children improved," which laundering the one tested number (180k) into the largest announced one (1.38m). They are not the same currency. The measured number passed an outside test; the announced numbers count reach and self-assessment and have not.

## The rule

1. **Lead with the independently-measured number; label everything else "programme-reported."** In print, say which body measured what. Egypt piece: *"those numbers may every one be true, but they are programme-reported, not independently evaluated … to add the 180,000 that was measured to the half-million and the 1.3 million that were announced … is a category error."*
2. **Never sum or reconcile across currencies.** Measured + announced is not a bigger measured number; it is a measured number and a separate claim. State both, keep them apart, name the seam.
3. **The honesty is itself the story.** The discriminator (which figure a system *leads with*) is more revealing than any single figure — it is a transferable take ("a system is known by the figure it agrees to have checked"). Don't bury the seam in a footnote; make it the spine if the gap is wide.

## Companion sub-rule — date recirculated baseline statistics

A crisis baseline is often an **older figure recirculated as if current.** Egypt's "nearly six in ten / more than half of ten-year-olds cannot read a simple paragraph" is **learning-poverty** data (World Bank/UNESCO, SE.LPV.PRIM), drawn from international assessment *years before* the 2025 programme — and the World Bank's post-pandemic global update puts the figure nearer **seven in ten** (57% pre-pandemic → ~70% by 2022). UNICEF's "more than half" and the WB's "~70%" are the *same kind* of number at different vintages and adjustments.

Rule: when a programme's own comms cite a baseline crisis figure, **find the underlying measurement's date and present it as a standing baseline, not a fresh result.** Egypt piece: *"that figure is a standing baseline, drawn from international assessment in the years before the programme this piece is about. It is the crisis, not its verdict."* Give the honest range where two credible vintages disagree, attributed.

## Why it matters

This pattern recurs on **every government-flagship piece** — programmes are structurally incentivised to announce big and measure small, and to recirculate the worst baseline as current. The rule lets Madār use the programme's own numbers without becoming its press office, and converts a sourcing hazard into the piece's most durable idea.
