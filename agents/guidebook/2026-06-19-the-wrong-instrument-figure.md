# The wrong-instrument figure — when a real number cannot judge the policy it is aimed at

**Logged:** 2026-06-19 · **Surfaced by:** Philippines MTB-MLE reversal (sprint piece 6). · **Owner:** Researcher (recon) + Editor (verdict).
**Status:** durable ruling — banked as **§3 ruling #10**, the fourth dimension of figure discipline (#4 pin-to-scope, #9 measured-vs-announced, **#10 right-instrument**).

## The trap

A policy fight forms around a single dismal number, and both sides reach for it. In the Philippines reversal of mother-tongue-based instruction, that number is **PISA 2022 reading = 347** (vs OECD 476; ~24% at baseline proficiency). It is real, primary (OECD), and severe. It is *also the wrong instrument for the question being asked of it*:

- **Population mismatch:** PISA tests **15-year-olds**. The policy under dispute governs **kindergarten–Grade 3**.
- **Timing mismatch:** those 15-year-olds sat the test **years after** their mother-tongue grades ended — and many predate full MTB-MLE rollout entirely.
- **Mechanism / language mismatch:** PISA is sat **in Filipino and English**, not the home language whose use is the very thing in dispute.

So PISA can establish *that* Philippine reading is in crisis. It **cannot**, by its own design, show that the mother-tongue policy **caused** the crisis, or that **ending** it will cure it. Yet abolition-side and keep-it-side both cite it as proof. A number can be entirely accurate and still be evidence for **neither** proposition in the argument it has been drafted into.

## The rule

Before any figure is allowed to carry **causal or adjudicating** weight in a policy dispute (not merely descriptive weight), check three matches against the policy itself:

1. **Population** — does the metric measure the people the policy acts on? (PISA: 15-year-olds vs a K–3 policy → fail.)
2. **Timing** — was the metric measured *after enough exposure* to the policy to reflect it, and not before rollout? (PISA cohort largely pre-/under-exposed → fail.)
3. **Mechanism** — does the metric test through the channel the policy changes? (PISA tests in the non-home languages → fail on the home-language question.)

If any match fails, the figure is **descriptive, not dispositive**. Write it that way *in print*: name the crisis with it if you like, then say plainly why it cannot settle the policy question — and **refuse to let either side use it as proof**. The refusal is the reporting; it is exactly the move a partisan brief will not make, and it is what lets a contested piece be trusted (and forwarded) by both sides.

## Why it is distinct from #4 and #9

- **#4 (pin every figure to report/cycle/scope)** guards against *misreading* a number (wrong year, wrong sample, two studies one name).
- **#9 (measured-vs-announced)** guards against *summing across currencies* — reach vs independent measurement of the **same** programme.
- **#10 (right-instrument)** guards against a *correctly-read, single* number being applied to a question its design cannot answer. The number is right; the **inference** is the error.

The three compound: a figure must be read correctly (#4), kept in its own currency (#9), **and** be the right instrument for the claim (#10) before it bears weight.

## Companion observation — the reversal-honesty frame

This is the second statutory **reversal** in the catalogue (after Korea AIDT) and the contrast case in the **language-of-instruction cluster** (Iqra / Egypt build a home-language bridge; the Philippines legislates one optional). On contested reversals the durable stance is: **frame as a dispute, not a verdict; give both camps their strongest honest case; locate where the question will actually be settled** (here, the law's own three-year review — so the real test is whether the system *measures the children it just moved*, the #10 discipline turned forward). The recon's "second-editorial-read for sensitivity" flag is discharged by holding that frame, not by softening either side.

## Pre-stage check (add to the verdict routine)

> For each load-bearing figure used to argue *for or against* a policy: does it match the policy's **population, timing, and mechanism**? If not → mark it descriptive, state the mismatch in print, deny it to both sides as proof.
