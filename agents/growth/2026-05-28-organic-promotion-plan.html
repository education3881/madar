Organic Promotion Plan · No Spend

How Madār grows without buying anyone's attention

A no-money, non-aggressive plan for getting Madār in front of educators, parents, and education regulators. Six channels, one routine, and a list of moves we will refuse to make.

The frame

Editorial publications do not grow on marketing budget. They grow on fit, citation, and the small set of readers whose own credibility is on the line when they share something. The no-spend constraint is not a limit on Madār — it is what forces the only kind of growth that compounds. A publication that buys its first thousand readers has to keep buying. A publication that earns them keeps them.

Madār's posture is the campaign. Slow, sourced, bilingual, geographically honest. Every move in this plan is downstream of one principle: the work is the lead.

Four levers, in order of leverage

01 · Source reciprocity

Every named institution is a distribution channel

When a Madār piece names a real institution — BQA Bahrain, EKB Egypt, Madrasa UAE, the Bo school in Sierra Leone — we send the comms or press office a one-line courtesy email with the link in English and Arabic. We do not ask them to share it. They have audiences who care about being seen carefully, and they will share what is worth sharing.

Why it works: a one-line email reads as collegial, not promotional. The institution's amplification carries more credibility with their audience than any third-party endorsement we could buy.

02 · Substack network gravity

Sit inside the platform, not at its edge

Substack rewards reciprocity. We turn on Recommendations only for publications whose editorial standards we admire (geopolitics, education policy, MENA literature). We leave thoughtful comments on overlap publications when the comment genuinely belongs. We pitch one cross-post per quarter to a publication where the audience overlap is real.

Why it works: Substack's recommendation engine is the most efficient organic distribution channel for a slow editorial publication. It costs nothing and compounds with editorial reputation, not ad spend.

03 · The field-note card

One craft move, repeated per piece

For each piece, the Designer produces one square card and one vertical card — JetBrains Mono kicker, pull-quote in Cormorant, one source line. Posted to Instagram, X/LinkedIn, and the Substack notes feed. We do not boost; we do not engagement-bait. The card is itself the editorial signal: when people see the typographic language, they recognise the publication.

Why it works: a recognisable typographic signature is the cheapest brand-recall mechanism that exists. Hundreds of impressions a week with no spend, no algorithmic dependence on a single platform.

04 · Bilingual underservice

Arabic teacher audiences are undercovered

Arabic-language coverage of MENA education from a sources-verified, slow-cadence publication is rare. AR Twitter/X has a serious teacher community that the international education press does not reach. Madār's AR pieces are not translations alone — they are first-class citizens. Growth posts the AR card alongside the EN card, in the AR-native typographic system, every time.

Why it works: AR pieces are disproportionately shareable because the supply is thin. One Arabic teacher in Cairo who shares one piece reaches a network English-language outlets cannot touch.

The per-piece routine

Six moves, runnable in about 30 minutes per piece, kicked off the day a piece is approved. Growth owns the checklist. The Manager checks it ran before declaring the issue published.

  1. 01
    Generate two cards (square + vertical). Same typographic system as the piece. One pull-quote, one source line. Saved to /agents/growth/cards/<slug>/.
  2. 02
    Post the piece to Substack as the primary publication. EN goes to the main publication; AR goes to the AR-section. Title, dek, hero still, body. No additional commentary in the Substack post — the piece is the post.
  3. 03
    Post one X/LinkedIn thread. Three posts: the pull-quote, the one specific institutional fact, the link. Tag the named institutions. No hashtags except #Madar and the country tag (e.g. #Egypt).
  4. 04
    Send the courtesy email to named institutions. Three sentences. "We covered your work in our publication this week. Both languages. Sharing the link for your awareness." No ask. Sent from education3881@gmail.com.
  5. 05
    Submit the piece to one curated context. Rotates each week: a relevant subreddit (only where contributing genuinely), the Substack "Education" leaderboard, an open call from an overlap publication. Never spammy.
  6. 06
    Update the publication's "Issues" page. The piece joins the back catalogue with a one-line scoping summary. This is how the publication's growing surface area becomes a research artefact in its own right.

Cadence over 90 days

A slow publication moves slowly on every axis. The cadence here mirrors the editorial cadence — small consistent moves, never a campaign push.

Weekly One AR-only field note on X/Substack — a single quote from a published piece, no link, no ask. The publication speaking Arabic to Arabic readers without translating itself. Audience signal: we are not a translation outlet.

Ongoing Comment scholarship. When a major outlet covers MENA/African education thinly, the Editor leaves one substantive comment, with a Madār link only if it would actually serve the reader. Editorial generosity is the cheapest reputation moat.
What Growth will not do
  • Send outreach DMs to strangers — the publication does not cold-pitch.
  • Buy newsletter swaps, paid placements, sponsored posts, or any boosted social.
  • Run engagement-bait posts ("Hot take:", "Unpopular opinion:", number-bait, mystery hooks).
  • Publish AI-summary content, listicles, or "5 things you need to know" pieces.
  • Farm SEO with thin posts, keyword-stuffed pages, or AI-generated abstract round-ups.
  • Buy followers, signups, or email list expansions.
  • Run pop-ups, modal subscribe prompts, or any interstitial that interrupts reading.
  • Reference the country scope publicly — the editorial spine is internal; posture is signalled through what we cover, never through naming what we don't.

What success looks like at 90 days

No vanity metrics. The signals worth watching are the ones that prove the editorial bet, not the ones that prove activity.

01 · Reach
Substack organic subscribers (excluding those acquired by recommendation).
02 · Endorsement
Recommendations received from peer publications. Three is the target.
03 · Institutional pickup
Named institutions sharing or republishing pieces. Count, not vanity.
04 · Fit signal
Average time-on-page above six minutes; AR readers outperforming EN readers per page.

If we run the routine for ninety days and only one of those four metrics is healthy, the plan worked. If three are healthy, we are on the curve. The frame is not "how many readers" — it is "is the publication being read by the right hundred people."
