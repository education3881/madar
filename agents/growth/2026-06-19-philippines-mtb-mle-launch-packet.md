## 1. Why this piece travels

The share trigger is the *frame*, not the country. "Philippines scraps mother-tongue policy" is the wire story; everyone has it. Madār's angle is the discipline underneath: **both camps reach for the same failing PISA score (347), and that score cannot, by its own design, settle the argument** — it tests 15-year-olds, in Filipino and English, years after the K–3 mother-tongue grades have ended. That is the measured-vs-announced ruling (guidebook #9) applied to a *reversal*, and it flatters exactly the reader who distrusts how education numbers get wielded in policy fights. It is also, deliberately, **not a verdict** — which is what lets it be forwarded by people on *both* sides of a genuinely contested issue, rather than only the side that already agrees.

This is the catalogue's **strongest EN organic-search candidate to date** (the mirror of Egypt, which was our strongest *Arabic* field): RA 12027 is live, contested, and being searched right now by teachers, administrators and researchers who want more than the wire summary.

## 2. The cluster — language-of-instruction is now a triptych, and the contrast completes it

The related rail seats the Philippines in the language-of-instruction cluster — **Iqra (RAK: build a bridge *from* the home language, and measure it) ↔ Egypt (national-scale bridge, independently measured) ↔ Philippines (a home-language bridge legislated, then made optional).** The first two are *construction* stories; the Philippines is the *contrast* — same question, opposite policy direction. A cluster with a contrast in it is far more shareable than three variations on a theme: it reads as a genuine argument, not a beat. A reader who arrived on Egypt now has a three-stop reading path, which is the return-rate play the charter leads with ("worth returning to," not raw reach).
