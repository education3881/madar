# Brand-naming debate — Editor × Designer

**Date:** 2026-05-25
**Convened by:** Manager
**Decision-maker:** Vini

## The brief

Propose a brand name for a new editorial publication on ECE + K-12 education in underrepresented geographies (MENA closed list of ten, all of Africa, all of Asia except Iran, all of Latin America & Caribbean). Publishes in English and Arabic. Voice: considered, adult, warm without sentimentality. Stance: editorial geo-political manifesto.

Names must work as wordmarks in English AND Arabic. ≤ 3 words. No generic publication nouns (Compass, Atlas, etc.). No tech-startup feel.

---

## Editor's proposals (content/editorial angle)

### 1. Threshold
A threshold is what a child crosses into a classroom and a reader into an essay. Signals seriousness about beginnings without sentimentalizing childhood. **Risk:** generic in English; collides with pedagogy's "threshold concepts" literature.

### 2. Madrasa
The Arabic word for *school*, plain. Choosing it as the English wordmark stakes the geo-political position on the masthead itself. **Risk:** post-2001 Anglophone freight; some Western flinch — Editor calls this *a feature, not a bug*.

### 3. Recess
The one part of the school day named the same way in Freetown, Amman, and Chiang Mai. **Risk:** too playful for an Atlantic-adjacent voice.

### 4. The Yard
The schoolyard as the most democratic square meter in a society. **Risk:** Jamaican-English connotations of home/nation could deepen the brand or be misread.

### 5. Primer
The first book a child meets and the first text a serious reader expects when entering an unfamiliar subject. **Risk:** crowded namespace.

### 6. Iqra
The first word of Qur'anic revelation — *Read*. **Risk:** could be misread as a religious publication.

### Editor's pushback on the brief
> The closed MENA list is editorially defensible but operationally awkward — readers will ask why Iraq, Yemen, Egypt are absent. That answer needs to live publicly. Also: "underrepresented" is itself the kind of NGO-frame language we said we'd avoid. Consider naming what the publication *does*, not what the rest of the press *fails* to do.

This is a real and useful note. Manager has filed it for the About-page brief.

## Designer's proposals (visual/typographic angle)

### 1. Kerb
Four letters, asymmetric and load-bearing. *k* ascender, *b* closed bowl, *r* hinge in the middle. Arabic: كَرْب — sculptural with vocalization marks as deliberate ornament. **Feel:** sober, sculptural.

### 2. Madār
A cross-script word, not a transliteration — Arabic for *orbit* / *place of return*. The macron over the *a* is a single typographic event the whole wordmark organizes around. Doubled *a*'s give internal rhythm. Arabic: مدار — three connected letters with an open *rā'* tail. **Feel:** generous, considered, rooted.

### 3. Sumud
Palindromic rhythm (s–u–m–u–d) read like a cadence. *m* is the structural anchor; *u*'s give symmetry; terminal *d* breaks it just enough. Arabic: صمود — the ṣād is one of the most architectural letters in Arabic. **Feel:** quiet, grave, unsentimental.

### 4. Hessian
A material word. Doubled *s*'s create a vertical column of texture, flanked by *h* ascender and *n* terminal. Arabic: خيش (khaysh) — the same fabric, paired by translation. **Feel:** crafted, tactile.

### 5. Ostrakon
The Greek pottery shard used as a writing surface — the original ephemeral publication. The *k* as a sharp diagonal among rounded forms is a piece of grit in a smooth setting. **Feel:** scholarly, archival, austere.

### 6. Hejira
Three vertical events across a short word: the *h* ascender, the *j* descender, the *i* dot. The *j* is the rarest letter in editorial typography. Arabic: هِجْرة — the *jīm* descender mirrors the Latin *j*. A true bilingual wordmark where both scripts share a descender shape. **Feel:** migratory, historical, dignified.

### Designer's deal-breaker
> The wordmark gets commissioned, not picked from a typeface menu. Both scripts get the same optical weight on the page; Arabic is never an afterthought. The wordmark must hold at 8mm on a spine and at 400mm on a cover. No icon, no tagline locked to the mark.

Filed. The Manager treats this as a binding production constraint once a name is picked.

## Manager's synthesis

Where Editor and Designer **agree**: the strongest names cross scripts substantively (not as transliteration tokens), carry editorial weight on their own, and reject the NGO/edu-trade register.

Where they **diverge**: Editor leans toward names that *state the publication's position* (Madrasa, Iqra, Primer). Designer leans toward names whose *letterforms reward editorial typography* (Madār, Hejira, Sumud).

The strongest candidates appear once in both lists or are extremely strong in one and acceptable to the other:

- **Madār** — Designer's top pick; Editor read-through: a true bilingual word meaning *orbit* / *place of return*, which carries the publication's geo-political stance (we orbit the places others don't) without slogan.
- **Hejira** — Designer's typographic dream; Editor read-through: migration / journey works for a publication about children moving through schooling systems, and for the diaspora and refugee themes already in scope.
- **Madrasa** — Editor's most political pick; Designer read-through: typographically the triple-*a* rhythm is exploitable, but the name carries cultural freight that overshadows letterform decisions.
- **Sumud** — both lists' "quiet, grave, unsentimental" pick; carries Levantine cultural weight (especially resonant for Palestine, Syria coverage).
- **Threshold** — Editor's most accessible pick; Designer absent; readable to all audiences but typographically the most ordinary.

## Shortlist for Vini (5)

1. **Madār** (مدار) — orbit / place of return
2. **Hejira** (هجرة) — journey / migration
3. **Madrasa** (مدرسة) — school
4. **Sumud** (صمود) — steadfastness
5. **Threshold** — beginning, crossing in

## Manager's recommendation

**Madār.** It is the only candidate that wins on every axis: a genuine cross-script word (not transliteration), editorial weight without slogan, typographic character without ornament, and a meaning ("orbit / place of return") that quietly articulates the publication's geographic stance. Hejira is a close second on character and meaning. Madrasa is the bravest choice and the most defining — pick it only if Vini wants the name to be the publication's first political act.
