## Candidate 1 — UAE × Madrasa platform, ten years on

**Form:** Curated · **Level:** K-12 (with ECE entry points) · **Region:** GCC

**Themes:** language/heritage preservation · AI-readiness · government-led programs

**Editorial angle.** The Madrasa platform (madrasa.org), funded by the Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum Knowledge Foundation, has been quietly translating and producing Arabic-language STEM and primary-curriculum content for almost a decade. The 2018 launch milestone — five thousand video lessons in Arabic, free to the region — was covered. The ten-year mark is unlikely to be covered with the same attention, and the more useful question is what the platform's catalogue actually demonstrates: not that Arabic-language educational technology can exist, but what it costs in editorial time, terminology decisions, and dialect-vs-fusha trade-offs to maintain it at the volume Madrasa now does. A careful reading of the platform's published curriculum standards and content-policy documentation, rather than the launch coverage.

**Primary source (Editor to verify in draft pass):** Madrasa platform content and curriculum framework documents at madrasa.org; cross-referenced against the Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum Knowledge Foundation's published reports on the initiative.

**Why this clears the conflict line:** Federal-level Knowledge Foundation initiative, not ADEK-specific. Vini's day job has no editorial overlap with Madrasa's curriculum production.

## Candidate 2 — Saudi Arabia × Human Capability Development Programme, education chapters at four years

**Form:** Curated · **Level:** K-12 · **Region:** GCC

**Themes:** government-led programs · AI-readiness · access and equity

**Editorial angle.** The Human Capability Development Programme (HCDP), one of the Vision 2030 execution programmes, published its initial framework in 2021 with substantial education chapters covering K-12 reform, teacher development, and what was then framed as "21st-century skills." Four years in, the programme has issued progress documents that read differently from the launch framing: more granular, more sober about timelines, and — interestingly — more willing to name specific subsystems that have not moved at the projected pace. A careful reading of the most recent progress document, particularly the education sections, would be the kind of work Madār is positioned to do: take a heavy, official document seriously and pull out what's actually changed in the language since 2021.

**Primary source (Editor to verify in draft pass):** Human Capability Development Programme, 2021 framework document (vision2030.gov.sa/en/explore/programs/human-capability-development-program/) and the most recent annual progress report from the programme office.

## Candidate 3 — Oman × Kindergarten expansion under Vision 2040

**Form:** Editorial (Field Note) · **Level:** ECE · **Region:** GCC

**Themes:** ECE access · sustainability · government-led programs

**Editorial angle.** Oman's 2040 Vision document and subsequent education-sector strategy documents include ECE expansion targets that have, in the last three to four years, moved from policy ambition to actual building of kindergarten capacity, particularly in the interior governorates. The piece would be a short editorial — not a curated reading of one document but a careful framing of what the build-out has been actually doing on the ground, drawing on Ministry of Education sector planning documents and recent reporting from Omani local press. The ECE register matters here: many GCC ECE expansion stories get told only in terms of private-sector growth (international curriculum kindergartens). The Omani public-sector ECE build is a different story and worth telling in that register.

**Primary source (Editor to verify in draft pass):** Oman Ministry of Education ECE strategy and Vision 2040 education chapter; Omani local-press reporting on kindergarten openings in interior governorates (Al-Dakhiliyah, Al-Dhahirah).

## Candidate 4 — Qatar × Qatar Academy primary schools and the Arabic-language curriculum question

**Form:** Curated · **Level:** K-12 · **Region:** GCC

**Themes:** language/heritage preservation · national identity · government-led programs

**Editorial angle.** Qatar Academy schools (the K-12 schools operated under Qatar Foundation, distinct from Education City's higher-education tenants) have been quietly revising their primary-school Arabic-language curriculum frameworks in ways that have not been widely covered. The interesting move is the decision, in the past two cycles, to treat Arabic as the medium of instruction for substantive subject content in early primary — not just as an Arabic-language class. A careful reading of the published curriculum framework documents, set against international curriculum schools' more typical English-first model, is the kind of piece that contributes a non-trivial argument to the bilingual-instruction conversation in the region.

**Primary source (Editor to verify in draft pass):** Qatar Foundation Schools primary curriculum framework documentation, available on the QF Schools / Qatar Academy public-facing pages.

## Candidate 5 — Senegal × Daara modernisation programme

**Form:** Essay · **Level:** K-12 (with ECE-aged children present in many *daaras*) · **Region:** Africa (the non-MENA slot)

**Themes:** respect for religion · language/heritage preservation · government-led programs · access and equity

**Editorial angle.** Senegal's longstanding programme to bring *daaras* (Quranic schools) into the formal education system — adding French, mathematics, and science instruction alongside the Quranic core, accrediting teachers, and providing standards-based oversight — is among the more significant and least-internationally-discussed education reforms of the past decade in West Africa. The story has been told in NGO registers ("modernizing traditional schools") that miss the politics, and in religious-conservative registers ("eroding the Quranic tradition") that miss the public-administration craft. An essay-length piece, in Madār's editorial voice, that takes both the religious tradition and the public-sector reform seriously without flattening either is the kind of long-form work Issue 02 could carry.

**Primary source (Editor to verify in draft pass):** Senegal Ministry of National Education's daara modernisation programme documentation; academic work on the programme published in the last five years (recommended starting point: Brookings Africa Growth Initiative working papers, AfDB sector reviews).
