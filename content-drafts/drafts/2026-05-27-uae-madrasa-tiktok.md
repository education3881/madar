## Architecture note to the Editor

The piece opens on the cooperation-agreement signing of 15 May 2025 — three named people in a room — and pulls back to what Madrasa actually is: a seven-year-old, free, Arabic-language K-12 STEM video platform with seven thousand interactive lessons. It places the platform's wager (Arabic-first STEM content, free, structured by grade) against the partnership's wager (algorithmic distribution through TikTok's STEM Feed) and asks what each party gave the other. The turn is on the editorial cost of routing curriculum through a feed the curriculum team does not control. It closes on the open question: did the platform expand its reach, or did it surrender the next paragraph that follows each of its videos.

**A brief correction folded in.** The slate the Editor inherited called this "Madrasa ten years on" and named the wrong parent (MBRF rather than MBRGI). The brief corrected both. The piece works with the right facts.

## Draft

On the 15th of May 2025, three people sat down in Dubai to sign a piece of paper that, read carefully, is the kind of administrative document Madār is in the business of reading carefully. Saeed Al Eter for the Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum Global Initiatives. Dr. Waleed Al Ali for Madrasa, the Arabic-language educational platform that MBRGI has been quietly funding and running since 2018. Kinda Ibrahim for TikTok's Middle-East-and-beyond operations. The cooperation agreement they signed routed Madrasa's catalogue — more than seven thousand interactive lessons across science, technology, engineering, mathematics, and Arabic-language studies, free at the point of use, calibrated from kindergarten to grade twelve — into TikTok's STEM Feed for users in the United Arab Emirates.

The press release that came out of the signing said what press releases of this kind say. The interesting thing is the document the press release is summarising.

Madrasa, in its own self-description on `madrasa.org`, is a video library. Sciences and mathematics, Arabised, from rounded primary-grade material through to the upper-secondary curriculum, delivered in Modern Standard Arabic with the kind of editorial consistency that — anyone who has tried to lecture a six-year-old in *fusha* will know — is harder than it looks. The platform was launched in October 2018. It is one of those institutions whose existence the international education conversation has not had a strong reason to remark on, because the international conversation about Arabic-language educational technology is, generally, not loud. Madrasa has been producing the Arabic STEM video most consistently of any free public-good platform in the region for the better part of a decade. The work is real, and it has been visible mainly to the people who have used it.

What changed on the 15th of May is the distribution layer.

The TikTok STEM Feed is a specific product. It is not "the TikTok algorithm" in general; it is a filter, available to users aged eighteen and above (with a toggle in content preferences), that biases the feed toward content TikTok's review pipeline has classified as STEM-educational. Common Sense Networks and Poynter are named in the cooperation agreement as the partners who do the quality and reliability vetting. Inside that feed, in the UAE, Madrasa's catalogue now flows. Dr. Al Ali's quote in the announcement is direct about what the platform thinks it is getting: *"Launching the largest free Arabic educational video library on TikTok marks a new phase for Madrasa in advancing self-learning and providing Arab students with essential knowledge. This initiative offers accessible, high-quality content through an innovative approach that blends structured learning with engaging presentation, leveraging the reach and appeal of social media and educational videos."* Kinda Ibrahim, on TikTok's side, said the regional appetite was clear: *"With nearly 10 million STEM-related videos shared globally on TikTok over the past three years, it's clear there's a strong appetite for educational content."*

Read the two sentences side by side and the trade becomes legible. Madrasa wants reach into a register — short-form, scroll-paced, in-line-with-the-feed — that its own website and its own YouTube channel do not natively provide. TikTok wants a high-credibility content vertical that elevates the STEM Feed above the suspicion that hangs over recommended-content channels in 2026. Each side gets something the other is positioned to provide. The platform extends its audience; the network gets a curriculum-grade backbone for a feature it has every commercial reason to make work.

The harder questions are downstream of the trade.

The first question is the question of context. When a Madrasa video is watched on `madrasa.org`, the next thing the viewer sees is another Madrasa video, organised by the platform's curriculum architecture — a grade, a subject, a sequence. When a Madrasa video is watched inside the TikTok STEM Feed, the next thing the viewer sees is whatever the algorithm has determined STEM-adjacent. Common Sense Networks and Poynter vet the content, per the announcement; they do not vet the *sequencing*. The sequencing is the algorithm's work, and the algorithm's incentive structure is not Madrasa's incentive structure. A curriculum platform's pedagogical claim is, in part, a claim about what comes next. Inside an algorithmic feed, the platform has handed the question of what comes next to a system that optimises for retention, not for learning.

The second question is the question of the under-eighteen viewer. The STEM Feed is, per TikTok's own announcement, available to users aged eighteen and above. Madrasa's catalogue is calibrated for kindergarten-through-grade-twelve students — most of whom, by definition, are under eighteen. The partnership routes a K-12 catalogue into a feed its target users cannot, by the platform's own age policy, switch on. The piece is not making this point as a gotcha. The point is that the partnership announces itself as a learning move while the access pathway is, by the partner's own rules, restricted to adults. The relationship between the catalogue and the feed deserves the question that the announcement does not raise: who, exactly, is the partnership building access for?

A note on what the partnership is not. It is not a privatisation of Madrasa. The platform remains free at its own URL, on its own terms, with its own curriculum architecture intact for any student who finds their way to it. The cooperation agreement is an addition to the distribution surface, not a substitution for it. Madār is not in the business of inventing villains where there is a real institutional decision to read carefully.

What the partnership does is move a question. Before May 2025, the question for Madrasa was how to grow an Arabic-language STEM library that students would reach for of their own accord. After May 2025, the question is how to keep the library's editorial logic legible inside a feed whose logic is not its own. The first question is answered by curriculum work; the second is answered by something the platform has not, in any document publicly available today, named.

The platform is, in 2026, seven years old. The TikTok STEM Feed is, in 2026, eighteen months old in the UAE. The question of what their alliance looks like at five years is not one a publication of this kind should answer. It is one the publication should mark.

— *The Editors*
