# About Madār

## What this is

Madār is an editorial publication about early childhood and K-12 education, written and made for readers who already take the subject seriously. We publish in English and in Arabic, with neither language treated as the translation of the other. The name means *orbit*: two languages held in one orbit, each with its own pull.

We are not a magazine of news, and not an aggregator. We publish slowly, in small quantities, and we expect each piece to repay close reading. A reader who comes for the speed of a feed is in the wrong place; a reader who wants to know what a primary teacher actually does on Monday morning, in a named school, in a named country, is in the right one.

## How we choose what to cover

The publication has an editorial posture, and it is the posture, not a geographic checklist, that determines what runs.

We are interested in education as it is practiced — by teachers, by ministries, by parents, by the people inside school buildings — rather than as it is theorized at conferences. We are interested in the texture of a system: the salary band, the textbook procurement cycle, the bus route, the cooling budget, the language of instruction debate, the headteacher who has been in the role for fifteen years and remembers three reforms. We are interested in policy when policy is the right unit of analysis, and we are not interested in it when the right unit of analysis is a classroom.

We follow stories that the international education conversation tends to discuss only in aggregate, or only when something has gone catastrophically wrong. A school year in which nothing collapses is, often, the story; a quiet reform that works is the story; a teacher's working week is the story. The publication exists in part because that kind of attention is rarely paid in either the English-language or the Arabic-language education press, and the absence is something we want to correct in small, specific ways — one piece at a time.

We publish bilingually because the readers we want already live bilingually, and because the same education argument carries differently in Arabic than it does in English. A piece about a UAE cooling budget reads one way in English and another in Arabic; both readings are real, and both belong on the page. The bilingual close — a recurring stylistic device where a piece moves into a second language for its final register — is one of the publication's signatures.

We work slowly because the alternative is to write the wrong piece quickly. A piece that needs another week to verify a salary figure gets another week. A piece that cannot pass the dignity check — the test of whether the people described would recognize themselves in our description of them — does not run.

## How we write

A few conventions are visible in the pieces we publish, and worth naming.

Pieces fall into three forms. *Curated* pieces are short readings of a single primary document or piece of local journalism, with our interpretation set against it — never a summary, never an aggregation. *Editorial* pieces are short original commentaries that argue a single claim from named sources. *Essays* are longer original works, weekly, with a thesis a reader could not have arrived at by skimming a press release.

Some pieces end in two languages. We call this the bilingual close: the English argument has been made, and the closing register moves into Arabic, or Krio, or Spanish, or whichever language the people in the piece actually live in. The close is not decoration. We use it when the second language carries something the English cannot, and we refuse it when it would only signal cultural sensitivity to a reader who needs reassuring.

A small number of our pieces use composite figures — invented stand-ins assembled from interviews and published reporting where no single person can be named for legitimate reasons. Where this is done, the name of the composite character carries a dagger (†) on its first appearance, and a short footnote at the foot of the piece, above the sources, names the convention. This is the lightest visible mark that still does the disclosure honestly, and it tells the reader, on the page, what they are reading.

Every piece names a specific place, a specific time, and at least one named human being whose presence is not decorative. Every piece cites at least one primary source — a ministry document, a named educator quoted in a named publication, peer-reviewed work, local reporting — with a working link. Wikipedia is a starting point and never a citation.

## What we refuse

A short register-list, written out so that no future contributor can claim it was not.

We do not write in NGO-frame language. People in our pieces have professions and names; they are not *voices*, not *beneficiaries*, not *the underserved*. We do not use *stakeholders* when we mean parents, teachers, ministers, or regulators, and we do not use *solutions* when we mean policies, programmes, or arguments.

We do not write the savior story. Pieces that frame local educators as needing rescue by international actors will not run, regardless of how moving the rescue is. We do not romanticize children — not their poverty, not their resilience, not their photogenic faces. The dignity check is the last thing every piece passes before it ships: would the people described recognize themselves?

We do not write edtech booster copy. Madār is not a buyer's guide for products and not a cheerleader for technology in classrooms, and pieces written in that register will be sent back. We do not write in the language of strategy consultancies; *best practices*, *scalable models*, *outcomes-driven frameworks* are not phrases this publication uses without irony. And we do not exoticize. Specificity is the cure for exoticism, and specificity is what this publication trades in.

## Who is making this

Madār is made by a small team led by its founder. Bylines on each piece name the editor and the designer for that piece. The founder's identity is not the publication's selling point; the publication is.

## A note on Arabic

Arabic is not translation. It is the second register Madār operates in, equal in weight to the English, and some pieces are Arabic-first in their conception while others are English-first. The bilingual close, when it earns its place, is the mechanism by which the two registers meet on the page. A reader of either language should leave a Madār piece feeling that the publication has paid them the same attention.
