# Jordan's double-shift schools, twelve years on: a ministry note worth reading slowly

**[PLACEHOLDER SOURCE — Editor to verify before publication.]** The primary source referenced below is described as a March 2025 progress note from Jordan's Ministry of Education on the double-shift school system. Editor to confirm the exact title, publication date, and URL before this piece runs. The framing in this draft holds regardless of which specific ministry document anchors it; what matters is that the source be an official Jordanian ministry communication, not an international agency summary of one.

The Hashemite Kingdom of Jordan has, since 2013, operated a quietly extraordinary administrative arrangement in a significant share of its public schools. The building opens at roughly seven in the morning for one cohort of students, who finish by midday. A second cohort, often Syrian children registered for the afternoon shift, arrives by around twelve-thirty and runs until roughly five. Two school days, one school building, one staff structure stretched across both. This is not a pilot. It has been the working arrangement for more than a decade in the schools where it operates.

The reason to point to the ministry's own note on this, rather than to one of the many international assessments of it, is that the ministry writes about the arrangement in a register the international literature mostly does not use. It writes about it as a long-running domestic policy that has acquired the texture of a permanent system — with the management, staffing, building-maintenance, and curriculum-pacing implications that any decade-old system acquires — rather than as an emergency response that is somehow still ongoing twelve years in.

That distinction is the editorial frame. The international literature on Jordanian education and the Syrian displacement tends to treat the double-shift system as a humanitarian fact: an admirable improvisation by a host country under strain. The ministry's note treats it, correctly, as a piece of Jordanian public administration, with Jordanian civil servants, on a Jordanian budget line, serving Jordanian and Syrian children both. Those are different framings, and the second one is the one that respects the people doing the work.

Three excerpts from the note, in our reading, are worth pulling out.

The first is the section on what the ministry calls *تآكل المباني* — building fatigue. The note observes, in plain language, that a school operating for ten hours a day rather than six wears down at a different rate than the design assumptions of the original facility allowed for. This sounds banal until one notices what it implies: that the cost of the arrangement is partly carried, year by year, by the physical infrastructure itself, and that the bill comes due eventually in renovation budgets that did not exist when the policy began.

The second is the paragraph on teacher workload. The ministry notes that a non-trivial share of the teaching workforce in double-shift buildings has, over the years, taken on both shifts. The note is unusually direct that this is not the design intent of the system and that the ministry is studying its effects on teacher health and instructional quality. Direct acknowledgment of a known compromise, in a ministry document, is rarer than it should be and worth marking.

The third is the closing section on what the note calls — and we are translating loosely — the question of *دوام الترتيب المؤقت*, the permanence of the temporary arrangement. The ministry writes that twelve years constitutes, by any reasonable definition, a structural feature of Jordanian schooling rather than a contingency, and that the policy framework around it should be updated accordingly. This is the sentence we found most striking in the document. It is a ministry, in writing, retiring a phrase ("temporary") that the international system has continued to apply.

What is missing from the note, and worth interrogating: there is no disaggregation of learning outcomes between the morning and afternoon shifts that we could find in this document. That disaggregation exists, in academic work and in some donor-funded assessments, but the ministry's own note does not engage with it directly. Whether that is a methodological choice (the ministry does not consider the comparison fair) or a political one (the ministry does not wish to publish the gap) is, in our view, the most important question a careful reader of this document is left with.

Read the note in Arabic if you can; the English version, where available, smooths some of the more direct phrasing.
