The Escuela Nueva model — Colombia's homegrown approach to one-teacher, multi-grade rural primary schools, developed from 1975 onward — turned fifty in 2025. The anniversary was marked, predictably, by a wave of international coverage rehearsing the same three or four claims that the model has been credited with since the World Bank began writing favorably about it in the late 1980s.

A more useful document, in our reading, came not from the foundation that holds the model's intellectual property but from a Colombian rural teachers' association, which issued a statement in October 2025 that does something the anniversary coverage almost entirely failed to do. It addresses the model from the position of the people who have actually been delivering it, in *veredas* across Cauca, Boyacá, and Nariño, for the past three to five decades, and it makes a series of corrections to the public record that we think serious readers of education policy should be aware of.

We will pull three.

First, on the question of replicability. The international literature has tended to describe Escuela Nueva as a model that has been "scaled" or "exported" to dozens of countries. The teachers' association is more precise: what has been exported is the curricular materials and the classroom organization principles; what has not exported, and largely cannot, is the specific Colombian rural-teacher training pipeline, the local-government supervisory relationships, and the community management committees that the model was designed around. The statement puts this in a line worth quoting in full, in our loose translation: "The materials traveled. The conditions that made the materials work did not."

Second, on the role of the teacher. Much of the celebratory coverage of Escuela Nueva, particularly in the English-language education press, has framed it as a "student-centered" model in which the teacher steps back. The teachers' association takes issue with this framing on two grounds. One, it understates what the multi-grade teacher actually does, which is considerably more cognitively demanding than running a single-grade classroom. Two, it has been used, over the years, as justification for understaffing rural schools — on the grounds that the model is supposedly designed to function with minimal teacher intervention. The statement is sharp on this point.

Third, on the question of what fifty years has actually demonstrated. The association's statement does not claim that Escuela Nueva has solved rural primary education in Colombia. It claims, more carefully, that the model has produced, across half a century, a body of practical knowledge about multi-grade teaching in conditions of geographic dispersion and uneven state presence — and that the next fifty years of work, in their view, should be about codifying that knowledge before the cohort of teachers who carry it retires.

What is missing from the statement, and worth interrogating: there is no engagement with the question of how Escuela Nueva intersects with Colombia's post-conflict rural reconstruction agenda, which has had its own implications for rural schooling since the 2016 peace accord. Whether the omission is strategic (the association does not wish to be drawn into national-political framing) or simply outside the scope of the statement, we cannot say from the document alone. It is the most significant absence we marked.

The reason to read this statement now, rather than the anniversary coverage, is that the statement was written by people who would still be teaching in these schools next Monday morning if the international press lost interest in the model tomorrow. That is not a small qualification on whose account of fifty years is worth the reader's hour.
