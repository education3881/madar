## The piece

Egypt's national digital education platform — the **Egyptian Knowledge Bank (EKB)** — turns ten in 2026. It is one of the largest publicly funded education and research access systems in the world: free at the point of use to every Egyptian citizen, federated with a long list of international publishers (Britannica, Elsevier, ProQuest, Cambridge), with a parallel platform aimed at K-12 students (study.ekb.eg).

In June 2025 the platform crossed an interesting threshold: it became one of the first national-scale education infrastructures to ship **generative-AI tools as a free public good** for its researcher cohort, via an integration with Elsevier's Scopus AI, ScienceDirect AI, and Reaxys.

The piece is a slow reading of **what it means for a state of 110 million people to commission, fund, and run — for a decade — a free national knowledge platform, and now to put AI tooling inside it.** It is not a celebration. It is not a critique. It is a reading.

The frame Madār is interested in: this is the largest non-Western experiment in publicly funded national-scale knowledge infrastructure, and the international education conversation talks about it less than it talks about the OECD's PISA results. The publication is partly in the business of correcting that.

## The frame — what this piece is *for*

1. **Sketch what the EKB actually is.** Most international readers — and most regional readers too — do not know. Be concrete: free at point of use, federated, includes both research-grade content (for universities and researchers) and K-12 content (study.ekb.eg). Give us numbers where the sources name them.
2. **Locate the decade-old moment.** EKB launched in 2016. What was the case made for it then, and what is the case for it now? The piece must surface at least one quote from the period of its founding — from Egyptian press, government materials, or UNESCO's case-study documentation.
3. **Read the AI integration.** From June 2025, EKB ships Elsevier's AI tools to a public researcher cohort. Read this carefully: who does it serve, what does it cost, what does it commit the state to over the next five years (licensing, training, governance)?
4. **End on the open question.** Madār is interested in the question, not the answer. The open question for the close: when a state commits to running national knowledge infrastructure for a decade and then routes its AI through commercial Western publishers, what is the long arc — sovereignty, dependency, or both?

## What this piece is NOT

- A press-release write-up of the EKB or the Elsevier deal. We are not Britannica's communications team and we are not Elsevier's either.
- A "developing country builds AI tools" story. The publication does not write inside that frame and the brief does not allow it.
- A piece on Egyptian politics. The EKB persists across changes in government; the piece is about an institution, not a regime.
- A piece on higher education. Higher education is out of scope (`/docs/10_geo_scope.md`). The frame is the K-12 dimension (study.ekb.eg) and the implications for the national education system more broadly. The research-tier and AI integration are scene-setting, not the centre.
