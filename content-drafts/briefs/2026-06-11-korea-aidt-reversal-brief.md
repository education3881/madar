## The angle, in one sentence

In eighteen months South Korea introduced AI-driven textbooks into classrooms at national scale, watched teachers, parents and its own legislature push back, and then un-decided them — the National Assembly narrowed the legal definition of "textbook" to exclude them (4 August 2025) — making Korea the first system in the world to run the *full* arc, adoption to statutory reversal, and therefore the first one others can actually learn from.

## Why this piece, why now

Madār already carries the two hopeful poles of the national-AI-in-education story: Uruguay (build a lab first, evaluate before deploying) and Indonesia (make it an elective, let schools opt in). Korea is the missing third pole — deploy first at scale, evaluate in public, reverse by statute. The 2026 state (adoption discretionary, ~30% of schools, subscription funding withdrawn, publishers stranded) is current, not stale; the un-deciding is *still being paid for*, which is exactly the administrative document Madār reads carefully. The piece is NOT "AI in classrooms failed" — it is about what a reversal costs, who pays it, and what it means that a democracy could execute one at all.

## The spine (corroborated across independent press; figures NOT yet primary-traced)

- **Sept 2024:** Ministry of Education approves **76 AI textbook titles** (operator of record for infrastructure: KERIS). Architect on the record: then deputy PM / education minister **Lee Ju-ho**, who framed AIDT as personalised-learning policy.
- **March 2025:** AIDTs enter classrooms with the school year (maths, English, informatics first wave).
- **One semester of friction:** documented complaints — factual inaccuracies, data-privacy exposure, screen-time, heavier (not lighter) teacher workload.
- **3 June 2025:** Lee Jae-myung, who campaigned explicitly on rescinding AIDT's textbook status, wins the presidency.
- **4 August 2025:** the National Assembly passes the amendment narrowing "textbook" to printed books and e-books, excluding "learning support software using intelligent information technology." AIDTs become **supplementary materials** — adoption at each school's discretion, central subscription funding gone.
- **2026 state:** adoption ≈ **30%** of schools; schools that complied early now carry subscription costs unaided; private publishers' investment (press figures range to ~$580M) stranded.

## The inconvenient half (mandatory — and note it runs in BOTH directions)

This piece is structurally critical of a rollout, so the inconvenient half is the **steelman of the program**, not another grievance:

1. **Process failure is not concept failure.** The objection record is dominated by speed, mandate and a thin evaluation base — not by evidence that adaptive software cannot help. Korea ran no Uruguay-style trial first; the absence of an evaluation is the failure, and that cuts at the *reversal* too (the legislature also acted without one).
2. **Teachers are not anti-AI.** Korean teachers were simultaneously leading voluntary AI adoption elsewhere in the system (World Bank field reporting). The resistance was to the mandate and the workload, not the technology — flattening it to "teachers vs AI" would be false.
3. **The reversal has its own casualties.** A third of schools adopted in good faith and are now defunded mid-subscription; publishers invested against a government signal that then inverted. If the piece praises the democracy that can un-decide, it must also count what un-deciding costs and who was left holding it.
4. **Figure discipline:** the spend figures disagree across the press (≈$850M government spend in one tier of coverage; ₩1T ≈ $736M "training + infrastructure" in another; $580M private). They are NOT interchangeable and none is yet primary-traced.
