## The angle, in one sentence

Every Arabic-speaking child learns to read in a language nobody speaks at home — Modern Standard Arabic (*fuṣḥā*) sits a measurable distance from the colloquial vernacular of the playground — and a small, rigorously evaluated program in Ras Al Khaimah has now put a number on what closing part of that gap, deliberately, is worth.

## Why now

A 2024–25 randomised trial of the **IQRA** early-grade reading program in Ras Al Khaimah has just reported (covered May 2026), and RAK has decided to roll IQRA out across its private schools from **September 2026** (KG1–Grade 1). That is the hook: a government-adjacent system treating the diglossia gap as a design problem with an evaluated intervention attached — not a lament about *fuṣḥā* in the abstract. It rhymes structurally with the Sierra Leone piece (a public system that measured what its fix bought, and kept the inconvenient half in).

## The spine (corroborated across independent sources — see source index)

- **Diglossia is the structural fact.** Arabic children speak a colloquial vernacular (Egyptian, Gulf, Levantine …) but must learn to read in MSA. The distance impairs the foundational sub-skills of early reading: phonological awareness, decoding (letter→sound), lexical access, morphological awareness. This is settled in the research literature, not a Madār claim (Saiegh-Haddad 2025; Frontiers 2024/2025; multiple peer-reviewed studies).
- **The intervention is named and evaluated.** IQRA was developed by the **Sheikh Saud bin Saqr Al Qasimi Foundation for Policy Research**, designed with cognitive psychologist **Dr. Helen Abadzi**; the 2024–25 trial was run by **J-PAL MENA** across **83 classrooms in 26 schools**. Reported gains: ≈ **+25% of a school year** in reading; median student **50th→54th** percentile overall, **50th→58th** in word reading; measurable gains in letter identification, word reading, non-word decoding, oral reading fluency.
- **The cross-regional mirror is Egypt.** ~100M+ speakers of Egyptian Arabic; the same diglossic distance, the same early-literacy cost, but (so far) without the evaluated RAK-style intervention at national scale — Egypt began early-grade reading reform in 2010 and the debate is live (Egyptian Streets, March 2026). Egypt is the *why this matters beyond one emirate* lens, not a second case study.

## The inconvenient half (mandatory — the piece does not ship without it)

1. **The deficit-model dispute.** A live scholarly current argues diglossia should NOT be framed as a deficit to be "fixed" — the child's vernacular is a competence to build on, not an obstacle to clear (Frontiers 2025, "non-deficit model"; UNESCO mother-tongue position). The piece must hold the tension: IQRA's gains are real *and* the framing of the colloquial as a problem is contested. The honest version is "build the bridge from the language they have," not "correct the language they speak."
2. **Scale and durability.** 26 schools in one emirate is a trial, not a system. The percentile shifts are modest in absolute terms (50→54). As with Sierra Leone retention: the test is not September gains but whether they hold — and whether a private-school RAK result transfers to a public-school Cairo classroom of 50.
3. **Whose language wins.** Privileging *fuṣḥā* access can read as devaluing the mother tongue. The dignity move (Madār voice) is to treat the colloquial as the asset the research says it is.
