## The one-sentence assignment

Read, slowly and fairly, the GIS-supported preference-matching algorithm that Sierra Leone's Teaching Service Commission used to place teachers in its 2024/25 deployment cycle — the machine that decides who gets sent to a school like the one in Bo — and tell the reader what it actually did, what it could not do, and why a ministry in a country with too few teachers built it anyway.

## Why this piece, why now (the "why now" the rubric demands)

The anchor piece (2026-05-25, Bo) is a teacher rationing last term's chalk in an under-resourced remote school. This is the *system* that decides who gets posted to schools like that one — the same problem viewed from the ministry, not the classroom. The "now": the first full deployment cycle to run on the algorithm (2024/25) has just produced reported outcomes, the policy that frames it (the NDLS 2025–2030) was published 1 January 2025, and a named TSC operational director has gone on the record about what changed at the classroom tier. The piece is a field-note-meets-the-policy-that-answers-it — a pairing only Madār is positioned to write, because it already owns the ground-level texture.

## The angle (hold this line)

The **algorithm is the piece; the NDLS is the backdrop.** Do not write a paraphrase of a strategy PDF. Write the mechanism: a transparent, open-source matching algorithm — inspired by the Nobel-winning deferred-acceptance work used for medical-resident and health-worker placement — applied to the most stubborn equity problem in the system, which is that the schools that most need teachers are the ones teachers least want to be posted to. Then tell the truth about the gap between what the algorithm optimised and what the system could deliver.

## Mandatory: the gains-and-limits balance (this is the piece's spine, and Test 4)

A piece that reports only the gains is an NGO press release and will be parked. A piece that reports only the limits is cynical and will be parked. The piece holds both:

**The gains (from the 2024 deployment round, TSC — all verifiable, cite with the figure):** 2,341 teachers recruited; 100% of selected teachers passed the licensing exam; 64% deployed to schools with a pupil-to-payroll-teacher ratio above 100; 57% to disadvantaged districts; 54% matched to their stated preferred school; only 7% placed more than 5 km from their preferred option. A tiebreaker rule favours female teachers, addressing the underrepresentation of women on payroll in remote schools.

**The limits (also on the record — name them plainly):** too few candidates passed the licensing exam to fill the pool the algorithm was meant to draw from; data-integration gaps between the teacher-management information system (TMIS) and the school census; limited TSC digital capacity; weak initial communication to teachers about how the matching worked. The honest piece is the one where the gains and these limits sit in the same paragraph.
