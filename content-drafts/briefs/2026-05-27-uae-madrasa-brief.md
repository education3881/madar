## Correction to the Issue 02 slate (read this first)

The slate at `/content-drafts/_ISSUE_02_SLATE.md` Candidate 1 had two factual errors that today's source check exposed. The Content Creator should not repeat them.

1. **Madrasa launched October 2018, not in or near 2016.** The phrase "ten years on" in the slate's framing is wrong. The platform is roughly **seven** years old in 2026.
2. **Madrasa is operated by the Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum *Global Initiatives* (MBRGI), not the *Knowledge Foundation* (MBRF).** These are two distinct UAE entities. The slate confused them. The Content Creator must name the correct parent.

The piece does not run "ten years on Madrasa." It runs on a different and better "why now" — see below.

## The piece

On **15 May 2025**, Madrasa — the free Arabic-language K-12 STEM video platform — partnered with TikTok to launch the **TikTok STEM Feed** in the United Arab Emirates, routing Madrasa's catalogue (more than 7,000 interactive lessons across STEM subjects and Arabic-language studies) into TikTok's algorithmic feed for users in the country. The partnership was signed at a cooperation agreement attended by Saeed Al Eter (Assistant Secretary-General at MBRGI), Dr. Waleed Al Ali (General Coordinator of Madrasa.org), and Kinda Ibrahim (Regional General Manager of Operations for TikTok Middle East, Türkiye, Africa, Pakistan, and South Asia).

The piece is a careful reading of what it means for a state-affiliated, free, public-good educational platform — built since 2018 around a structured Arabic STEM curriculum from kindergarten to grade 12 — to route its content through the world's most influential short-video algorithm. It is not a celebration of the partnership. It is not a critique. It is a reading.

The frame Madār is interested in: when a curriculum platform pushes its catalogue into an algorithmic distribution layer it does not control, the platform has, on quiet inspection, made an editorial decision about whose attention matters most. That decision is worth a question that the cooperation-agreement press release does not ask.

## The frame — what this piece is *for*

1. **Sketch what Madrasa actually is.** Free at point of use, Arabic-language, K-12 STEM + Arabic-language studies, more than 7,000 interactive lessons. Operated under MBRGI (the right parent name). Launched in October 2018.
2. **Locate the platform's quieter argument.** Madrasa exists in part because the Arabic-language educational-video catalogue available to K-12 students seven years ago was much smaller than the English-language one, and the platform's wager — that producing primary-grade-through-twelfth-grade STEM content directly in Arabic, free, would shift what students reach for first — was a long bet on a specific kind of access.
3. **Read the May 2025 TikTok partnership.** What does the STEM Feed promise the platform? What does it cost, by way of editorial control over the surrounding context (the next video the algorithm serves)? Who benefits — the platform, the algorithm, the student, all three?
4. **End on the open question.** When a curriculum platform partners with an algorithmic feed it does not control, has it expanded distribution, or has it surrendered the next paragraph?

## What this piece is NOT

- Not a press-release write-up of the partnership. We are not WAM and we are not TikTok's communications team.
- Not a "Arab youth and AI" piece, not a "social media is good/bad for learning" piece. The publication does not write inside those frames and the brief does not allow them.
- Not a piece on UAE politics or the Dubai Ruler's office. The platform persists across announcements; the piece is about a curriculum decision, not a leadership profile.
- Not a piece that uses the word "ambitious" of the partnership. Describe what it does and let the reader decide.
- Not a higher-education piece. Madrasa is K-12 (kindergarten to grade 12); that is its scope and ours.
