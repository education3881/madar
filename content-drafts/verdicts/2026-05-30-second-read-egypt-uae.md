## Egypt — "Egypt's Knowledge Bank at ten, and what it now ships through the front door"

**Holds. Arabic Editor's source-language flag clears.**

The carried-forward flag from the AR Editor's 2026-05-29 full-pass — "verify originating language of all historical quotes before next Egypt piece" — pointed specifically at the 2015 Shawki quote on EKB's founding rationale. I re-opened the AUC News URL today (https://www.aucegypt.edu/news/stories/dean-shawki-egyptian-knowledge-bank-widen-research-education-opportunities, published 11 November 2015). The quote in the piece — *"By providing these materials free of charge, the knowledge bank ensures that all Egyptians, no matter what their economic circumstances, will have the tools they need to excel in their education and research."* — appears verbatim in the AUC English-language story, attributed to Shawki on the public record at the time of the unofficial launch. **Not a back-translation. Sourced from an EN-original primary.** The AR version of the piece, accordingly, is the translation; the EN is the source. Flag closed.

The Ashour quote on the 2025 Elsevier integration — *"a leap forward for scientific advancement in Egypt"* / *"inspire innovation, nurture homegrown talent, and help Egyptian researchers take leading roles on the global stage"* — is from the Elsevier announcement page, which is an EN-original publisher-controlled document. Source is what the source claims to be: a ministerial framing on a publisher's marketing hub. The piece's editorial voice handles this correctly — the Elsevier executive's "deepening" framing is read alongside the minister's framing as the more revealing of the two, which is the right move and not snide.

**One sentence I would re-read tomorrow:** "A platform whose research tier ships a Western publisher's AI tools, and whose primary-school tier does not, has, on quiet inspection, made an editorial decision about whose access matters most at this moment." This is the piece's argumentative center, and it would survive a senior Egyptian education official's read because it is a structural observation about a real asymmetry in the June 2025 announcement — not a claim of bad faith. A hostile reader from the Ministry of Higher Education would respond by either contesting the asymmetry on the record or by addressing it. The piece earns the right to ask.

**Watch:** the EKB user-base figures (250k e-books, 1.5m theses, 7k journals, ~50 publishing houses) are sourced to "as of last year" rather than to a specific report URL. On a hostile read this is the softest line in the piece — the figures are credible and consistent with the UNESCO Gateways material, but a senior EKB administrator could ask which exact public document we drew them from. Flag for the next Egypt piece: when citing aggregate platform figures, link the specific annual report or fiscal-year readout.

## UAE — "The Arabic STEM library that decided to live inside the algorithm"

**Holds with one carry-forward note (no correction required).**

The three named-quote chain (Al Eter, Al Ali, Ibrahim) is sourced to the 15 May 2025 WAM/GDN press apparatus. The Al Ali quote on "launching the largest free Arabic educational video library on TikTok" and the Ibrahim quote on the ~10 million STEM-related videos shared globally over three years both appear verbatim in the source. The Common Sense Networks / Poynter vetting attribution is consistent with TikTok's STEM Feed product page.

The piece's risk class was always: did we sound like we were endorsing TikTok as an education platform. On this read the piece does the opposite of endorsing — it stages the trade ("each side gets something the other is positioned to provide") and then turns to "the harder questions are downstream of the trade." The two downstream questions (sequencing/algorithmic incentives; the under-eighteen viewer paradox) are both real, both attributed to the announcement's own self-description, and neither is the kind of point that requires the piece to take a moral posture above the question. The hostile reader — a senior UAE Ministry official who funded Madrasa on Madrasa's own terms — would not find this insulting to Madrasa. The piece is, in fact, generous to Madrasa as an institution while being precise about what the partnership does and does not do.

**One sentence I would re-read tomorrow:** "Madār is not in the business of inventing villains where there is a real institutional decision to read carefully." This is the piece's editorial spine and an explicit disavowal of the gotcha posture. Hostile reader: an MBRGI communications head. Their read: this publication is taking the partnership seriously enough to ask questions the announcement skipped, and is honest about not pretending the partnership is something it is not. That is the read we want.

**Watch / carry-forward note:** the under-eighteen viewer paragraph poses the question — "who, exactly, is the partnership building access for?" — without resolving it. The non-resolution is correct (the announcement does not answer it; the piece should not pretend to). But a future Madrasa piece, or a follow-up if the partnership produces public data on actual viewer demographics by end of 2026, should return to this question with the data. **Logging this as an editorial calendar follow-up** rather than a correction to the live piece. The piece's value is in marking the question; a follow-up earns the right to answer it.
