## Kuwait — "The test, in Arabic"

**Holds.** The piece's spine is the named-quote chain (Faten Al Bader, Gad Elbeheri, Charles Haynes) attached to public primary sources (IDA 2013, CCET institutional pages, the four-test battery enumeration). I re-opened the IDA URL today; the Elbeheri quote ("one million dollar project that CCET has completed") matches the source verbatim. The naming of the test battery — Test of Phonological Processing for Children, Test of Orthographic Processing and Morphological Awareness for Children, Test of Reading and Spelling for Children, Test of Working Memory for Children — is specific, checkable, and not paraphrased.

**One sentence I would re-read tomorrow:** "The longitudinal record across Arabic-speaking children with specific learning disabilities — a category that for most of the twentieth century had to import its evidence base from English-language research — is being built quietly, one annual cycle at a time, in a small building in the Al Surra district of Kuwait City." The "small building" framing is true and humane, not condescending. It would survive a CCET director's read.

**Watch:** the regional-question close is forward-looking and explicit that adaptation outside Kuwait is "the work of years, not months." That register is right — the piece does not claim what CCET has not claimed for itself.

## Iraq — "The library reopened on a Saturday in February"

**Holds.** This was the watch-piece. The political register around the 2014-17 period uses "the city's occupation by Islamic State" / "Mosul University's Central Library, bombarded and burned during the city's occupation" — accurate, period-correct, and not euphemistic. The Janke quote ("It is a great pleasure to witness the reopening of this facility...") is rendered verbatim from the UNDP press release with the contextual gloss that it reads as "what a development bank says when a development bank says the right thing" — that gloss is the piece's editorial voice, attributed to no source, and it is the right move.

**One sentence I would re-read tomorrow:** "A library is not finished when its building reopens. It is finished — in the sense in which a working library is ever finished — when the librarians know where the books are." This is the piece's argumentative center. It earns its weight by attaching to the UNESCO sub-project (catalogue work, Training of Trainers at Ulster, October 2024). The structural claim about the 40% enrollment increase as the load-bearing detail is correctly attributed to Zena Ali Ahmad's own framing in the UNDP statement.

**Watch:** the closing question — "whether the forty per cent that came back in 2022 stays, builds, and uses it — is the question the next decade will answer" — is a real open question, not a rhetorical flourish. It would survive a Mosuli academic's read.
