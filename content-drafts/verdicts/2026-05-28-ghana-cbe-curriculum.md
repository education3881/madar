## Five-test pass

1. **Sourcing reality.** Pass. The Ghana News Agency report of 31 March 2026 (by Jesse Ampah Owusu) and the Luminos Fund blog of 10 April 2026 (by Nunya Apetsi) were both opened today. The three named-human verbatim quotes — Dr. Clement Abas Apaak, Mr. Daniel Kwesi Ashiamah, Osama Makkawi Khogali (UNICEF Ghana) — are verbatim from those sources. The 3,844-page materials count, the four-language list (Dagaari, Dagbani, Asante Twi, Ewe), the fifteen-language target, the 60-day phase structure, the 12,000 Luminos-touched children, and the 500,000 since-2015 reintegration figure are all directly traceable.
2. **Specificity.** Pass. Accra, 31 March 2026, the four specifically named Ghanaian languages, the three-phase structure with day-counts and hour-counts, the named institutional actors (CEA, NaCCA, Luminos Fund, UNICEF Ghana, FCDO). Irreducibly Ghanaian.
3. **Why now.** Strong pass. The 31 March 2026 launch is two months old. The seven-year gap between the 2019 national curriculum and the CBE update is the article's spine, and it works.
4. **Dignity.** Pass. Three named figures quoted verbatim from their own public statements. The CEA Executive Director gets two quotes (the piece is correct to weight him over the Deputy Minister, since he is the institutional voice). The line about the UNICEF representative's "generic" sentence is sharp but the piece immediately argues *why* the genericness is the strength, which makes it fair rather than dismissive.
5. **Long arc.** Strong pass. 1995 (CBE founding as School for Life) → 2015 (500,000-children figure) → 2019 (national curriculum) → 2021 (Luminos partnership) → 2026 (revised CBE launch) → 2027 (first cohort under new materials). The open question — whether the eleven remaining languages get the same treatment on a five-year timeline — is the right one.

## Voice notes

- "*That is a generic UNICEF sentence, and its genericness is its strength.*" — kept. This is a hard sentence to land; the piece argues for it in the next clause, which is what makes it fair. Watch for cumulative use of "*that is what X says when X says Y*" across the publication; this is the second piece (after Mosul) where the construction appears. Should not become a tic.
- The piece names the regions specifically — Ashanti, Upper West, Volta, Upper East, Savannah. The Ghanaian regional vocabulary is the right register for the country; the equivalent specificity in Iraqi pieces would be "Ninewa," "Anbar," "Babil." Hold this standard.
