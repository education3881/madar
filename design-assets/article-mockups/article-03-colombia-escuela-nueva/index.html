Escuela Nueva
at fifty.

A 2025 statement from a Colombian rural teachers' association marks half a century of the country's multi-grade primary model — and corrects, gently, several things the international press has been saying about it.

The Escuela Nueva model — Colombia's homegrown approach to one-teacher, multi-grade rural primary schools, developed from 1975 onward — turned fifty in 2025. The anniversary was marked, predictably, by a wave of international coverage rehearsing the same three or four claims that the model has been credited with since the World Bank began writing favourably about it in the late 1980s.

A more useful document, in our reading, came not from the foundation that holds the model's intellectual property2 but from a Colombian rural teachers' association, which issued a statement in October 2025 that does something the anniversary coverage almost entirely failed to do.1 It addresses the model from the position of the people who have actually been delivering it, in veredas3 across Cauca, Boyacá and Nariño, for the past three to five decades, and it makes a series of corrections to the public record that we think serious readers of education policy should be aware of.

We will pull three.

On replicability

The international literature has tended to describe Escuela Nueva as a model that has been "scaled" or "exported" to dozens of countries. The teachers' association is more precise: what has been exported is the curricular materials and the classroom organisation principles; what has not exported, and largely cannot, is the specific Colombian rural-teacher training pipeline, the local-government supervisory relationships, and the community management committees that the model was designed around.

"The materials traveled. The conditions that made the materials work did not." — Rural teachers' association · October 2025

The statement puts this in a line worth quoting in full, in our loose translation: "The materials traveled. The conditions that made the materials work did not." The sentence does not appear in any of the anniversary coverage we read.

On the role of the teacher

Much of the celebratory coverage of Escuela Nueva, particularly in the English-language education press, has framed it as a "student-centred" model in which the teacher steps back. The teachers' association takes issue with this framing on two grounds. One: it understates what the multi-grade teacher actually does, which is considerably more cognitively demanding than running a single-grade classroom — managing four or five simultaneous lessons, four or five sets of expected learning outcomes, and the social dynamics that come with mixing eight-year-olds and twelve-year-olds in one room. Two: it has been used, over the years, as justification for understaffing rural schools, on the grounds that the model is supposedly designed to function with minimal teacher intervention. The statement is sharp on this point. The framing was useful for ministries that wanted to fund fewer teachers, and the statement, written by teachers, takes the framing back.

On what fifty years has shown

The association's statement does not claim that Escuela Nueva has solved rural primary education in Colombia. It claims, more carefully, that the model has produced, across half a century, a body of practical knowledge about multi-grade teaching in conditions of geographic dispersion and uneven state presence — and that the next fifty years of work, in their view, should be about codifying that knowledge before the cohort of teachers who carry it retires.

That is a different claim, and a more useful one. It is also a claim the model's own foundation has been slower to make in public than its critics might assume.

What is missing from the statement

What is missing from the statement, and worth interrogating: there is no engagement with the question of how Escuela Nueva intersects with Colombia's post-conflict rural reconstruction agenda, which has had its own implications for rural schooling since the 2016 peace accord.4 Whether the omission is strategic (the association does not wish to be drawn into national-political framing) or simply outside the scope of the statement, we cannot say from the document alone. It is the most significant absence we marked.

Why we are running this now

The reason to read this statement now, rather than the anniversary coverage, is that the statement was written by people who would still be teaching in these schools next Monday morning if the international press lost interest in the model tomorrow. That is not a small qualification on whose account of fifty years is worth the reader's hour.

It also matters, in our reading, because the question the statement closes on — codifying the practical knowledge of the cohort before it retires — is the question every long-running, community-rooted education programme will have to answer at some point. Escuela Nueva is fifty. There are programmes elsewhere on the same trajectory that are forty, and some that are sixty. The Colombian teachers' framing of the question may not be the last word on it, but it is the most useful one we have read this year.

Lectura en español
A Spanish reading — the same argument, written for our Spanish-language readers. Not a translation; a parallel paragraph.

Cincuenta años bajo la montaña

El modelo de Escuela Nueva — la propuesta colombiana de aula multigrado, desarrollada desde 1975 — cumplió cincuenta años en 2025. La cobertura internacional del aniversario repitió, como suele suceder, las mismas tres o cuatro afirmaciones que el modelo viene acumulando desde finales de los años ochenta. El documento que aquí leemos no proviene de la fundación que custodia los materiales del modelo, sino de una asociación de maestros rurales colombianos que lleva tres o cuatro décadas enseñando bajo ese sistema en las veredas de Cauca, Boyacá y Nariño.

La asociación corrige el registro público en tres frentes: la idea de "replicabilidad" — los materiales viajaron, las condiciones que los hacían funcionar no lo hicieron; la idea del "maestro que se aparta" — un encuadre cómodo para los ministerios que querían financiar menos maestros; y la idea de lo que cincuenta años de práctica han demostrado realmente — no la solución de la educación rural, sino un cuerpo acumulado de conocimiento práctico que debe codificarse antes de que la generación que lo carga se retire.

Lo que el comunicado omite es también legible: no aborda la relación entre el modelo y la agenda de reconstrucción rural post-conflicto que abrió el acuerdo de paz de 2016. Si la omisión es estratégica o simplemente queda fuera del alcance del documento, no podemos decirlo desde el texto solo. Es la ausencia que más nos llamó la atención.
