The Twenty-Mile Walk
How Sierra Leone maps its early-childhood coverage.

In 2018, six of seventeen districts reported any publicly-funded ECE provision. By 2024, thirteen did. The averages flatter the country. The chiefdom-level picture is harder to read, and more interesting.

¹ MBSSE Annual School Census 2018, p. 47. Definition narrowed in 2021 to exclude unregistered community centres; Madār's analysis re-baselines the 2018 figure under the 2021 definition.— MBSSE, Freetown.

² Headcount per chiefdom — the smallest administrative unit Madār could obtain at chiefdom resolution without extrapolation.— Statistics Sierra Leone, 2024.

³ Conversation with K. Bangura, Head of ECE Directorate, MBSSE, by phone, March 2026.

⁴ A "twenty-mile walk" is the working figure ECE directorates use internally for the longest acceptable catchment radius in low-density chiefdoms. It is not a published standard.

When the Ministry of Basic and Senior Secondary Education published Sierra Leone's 2024 school census in February of this year, the headline number — 190 publicly-funded early-childhood centres across the country's seventeen districts — was a thirty-eight percent increase on 2018.1 National coverage looks like a steeper trajectory than it was, in part because the 2018 figure was counted under a more permissive definition than the one in force today.1 Re-baselined, the rise is still real: from approximately ninety to a hundred and ninety, give or take. The number is not the story.

The story is what the number conceals. A chiefdom in Bonthe, on the coast south of the Sherbro Estuary, currently records one centre serving a catchment that extends, by the chiefdom education committee's own working estimate, to a thirty-two kilometre radius. The MBSSE's internal directorate calls this "the twenty-mile walk." It is not a published standard. It is the figure officers use when they argue, in meetings in Freetown, about which chiefdoms have been served and which have been counted.4

The averages have improved because districts that were close to the national mean in 2018 have pulled ahead, and because four districts — Bombali, Bo, Kenema, and Pujehun — have added centres at a pace nobody at the directorate predicted at the start of the decade. The averages have not improved because the chiefdoms that were furthest from a centre in 2018 are, with three exceptions, the same chiefdoms furthest from a centre in 2024.

What the chiefdom map shows

Counted at district level, Sierra Leone's progress is monotonic. Counted at chiefdom level — the unit at which a parent decides whether their four-year-old can be enrolled — the progress is bimodal. Some chiefdoms have multiple centres; some have none, and have not had one at any point in the period this publication has been able to reconstruct.2

"We have not added a centre in this chiefdom since 2014. The walk to the nearest one is more than two hours each way, for a child." — Chiefdom education officer, Pujehun district · March 2026

The directorate is aware. K. Bangura, head of the ECE Directorate at MBSSE, told Madār in March that the next five-year plan, currently in draft, will be the first to use chiefdom-level catchment as a planning unit rather than district-level enrolment.3 The directorate expects the plan to be published before the end of the calendar year. What it will do that the previous plans did not is treat the unevenness inside each district as a planning constraint, not a reporting footnote.

Whether the plan succeeds will turn on a single operational question: whether Sierra Leone's existing community-school structures — many of them unregistered, many of them informal — can be brought into the official count without losing the local autonomy that has kept them open through two governments and one Ebola emergency. The directorate is not unanimous on this. The teachers we spoke to were less unanimous still.

The map above is one version of the country. A higher-resolution version — chiefdom by chiefdom, with the catchment radii drawn — runs to thirty-four pages. The MBSSE has agreed, in principle, to share that map with Madār for a follow-up piece in the autumn.

A note on the ratio

One number is worth lingering on. Across the 190 centres counted in 2024, the average student-teacher ratio sits at 31:1 — a figure flattened by Freetown, where the worst-staffed centres reach 48:1. The figure below shows the country's centres sorted into five ratio bands. Half of them sit above the international guidance ceiling for the age group, which the ministry's own draft plan acknowledges.

The plan, when it lands, will be one of the few ministry documents in the region this decade to publish chiefdom-level ECE figures alongside the district-level ones. Whether that resolution survives the next round of donor reporting — which has historically pushed back toward district-level aggregation — is, at the time of writing, the most useful question to keep an eye on.

Glossary

Chiefdom. The smallest unit of local administration in Sierra Leone. There are 190 of them.

Catchment radius. The distance a child reasonably travels to a school. The MBSSE's working figure for low-density chiefdoms is ~32 km — the "twenty-mile walk."
