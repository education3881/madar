The Civics Page
That changed three times.

A single page of Jordan's Grade 7 civics textbook has been rewritten three times in eleven years. The three versions answer the same question — what is a citizen — in three different countries.

¹ The 2014 edition opens the chapter with: "The citizen is the one who carries the duties of the Kingdom."— Grade 7 Civics, 2014, p. 42.

² The 2018 edition opens the same chapter with: "The citizen is the one who participates in the public life of the Kingdom."— Grade 7 Civics, 2018, p. 42.

³ The 2023 edition opens the chapter with: "The citizen is the one who knows their rights, their duties, and the institutions through which both are practised."— Grade 7 Civics, 2023, p. 42.

⁴ Conversation with R. Khoury, curriculum specialist, NCCD, Amman, by video, April 2026.

Three copies of the same textbook sit on the desk in front of us. They look almost identical — the same Ministry of Education shield, the same dimensions to the millimetre, the same modest cover stock that has been the house style of Jordan's National Centre for Curriculum Development (NCCD) for at least twenty years. The differences are inside. Specifically, they are on page 42, which in all three editions opens the chapter titled, in Arabic, "What is a citizen?"

The 2014 edition opens the chapter with one sentence: "The citizen is the one who carries the duties of the Kingdom."1 Duties first. The chapter that follows runs to nine pages and lists those duties: military service for men, taxation, civic order. Rights appear later in the book, in a different chapter, structured as a closing list.

The 2018 edition opens with a sentence the curriculum specialists rewrote during a six-month consultation that included, unusually, two rounds of feedback from a sample of Grade 7 teachers in Irbid and Zarqa.4 "The citizen is the one who participates in the public life of the Kingdom."2 Participation moves to the front. Duties remain. The chapter is no longer than the 2014 version, but it has been reorganized: rights and duties share the same opening pages, the language of "participation" carries the chapter's argument, and one new paragraph names the National Centre for Human Rights — a body that did not appear in the 2014 chapter at all.

The 2023 sentence

The 2023 edition's opening sentence is longer than its predecessors and more specific: "The citizen is the one who knows their rights, their duties, and the institutions through which both are practised."3 The chapter that follows it is fourteen pages — five longer than 2014. The new pages do not add new duties. They add named institutions: the National Centre for Human Rights, the Independent Election Commission, the Royal Hashemite Court's office of complaints, the municipal councils. Each is given a paragraph. Each paragraph ends with a working URL.

"Civics in Jordan used to be a list of duties. It is becoming a map of institutions. The change is not rhetorical." — R. Khoury, NCCD · Amman, April 2026

R. Khoury, a curriculum specialist at the NCCD who worked on the 2018 and 2023 revisions, told Madār in April that the shift between editions was deliberate and incremental.4 "Civics in Jordan used to be a list of duties," she said. "It is becoming a map of institutions. The change is not rhetorical."

Whether the change reaches classrooms is a separate question. Two Grade 7 teachers in Amman who agreed to speak to us on background reported that the 2023 edition is in use, but that the 2014 framing — duties first — remains the dominant teaching frame, in part because the available teacher training has not kept pace with the textbook revision cycle. One of them said it more directly: "The book changed in 2023. The lesson plan I inherited from my predecessor did not."

Three pages 42, side by side

Below, the three editions' page 42 are reproduced as line diagrams — the layout, the headers, the position of the opening sentence and the diagrams the page carries. The cover and the binding are nearly identical across editions; the page is where the country's working answer to a single question has actually moved.

What to watch

The fourth edition of the textbook is, according to NCCD's published revision cycle, scheduled for 2027. Madār has asked to see the draft chapter on citizenship when one exists. Three things are worth watching when it does. First, whether "duties" returns to the opening sentence, as it would if the political conjuncture changes. Second, whether the named institutions on page 42 multiply or thin out — the count is the most legible single signal of where the curriculum sits on the spectrum between civic literacy and civic participation. Third, whether the teacher-training cycle catches up. Three editions of a textbook are only three editions of a country if the people teaching from them have been brought along.

«التربيةُ المدنيّةُ في الأردنِّ كانت قائمةَ واجبات. هي تتحوّلُ إلى خارطةِ مؤسّسات.»
